Managing Operations — PVRO

Study guide and practice quiz built from Assessment 1 & Assessment 2 missed questions

Operations & Competitive Strategy
Flexibility as a competitive priority
Missed — Assessment 1

In operations management, companies compete along four main priorities: cost, quality, time, and flexibility. Flexibility is the competitive priority that captures a company's ability to adapt quickly to changing customer requirements — whether that means adjusting product designs, shifting production volumes, or handling unique requests on short notice.

Service quality measures how well a service is delivered. Customer satisfaction is an outcome — a result of performance, not a capability metric. Processing time measures speed and efficiency. Flexibility specifically answers: how easily can this organization change when requirements shift? A manufacturer that can retool its line from one product to another in hours has high flexibility. A company locked into a single configuration has low flexibility regardless of how precise or fast its work is.

Assessment 1 confusion: "Service quality measure" was selected. Service quality tells you how well something was done — not how adaptable the operation is. These are fundamentally different performance dimensions.
Customer benefit packages — core vs peripheral goods
Missed — Both Assessments

A customer benefit package (CBP) is the complete bundle of goods and services a customer receives. At the center is the core product. Surrounding it are peripheral items that add value and help differentiate the seller. The critical distinction is between peripheral goods (physical, tangible items) and peripheral services (intangible support activities).

Financing options and online customer support are services — intangible and not physically part of the package. Software preloaded on a device is part of the core product experience — it is embedded in what you are buying, not added on top. A physical accessory like a replacement battery or charger is a true peripheral good: it is tangible, physically separate, and including it creates a differentiated offer a competitor might not match.

Both assessments confusion: Preloaded software was selected. Because it ships as part of the product's core functionality, it is not a peripheral good. Peripheral goods must be physically separate items that add value beyond the main product.
Customer ratings and future purchase behavior
Missed — Assessment 2

Customer ratings are most strongly correlated with future purchase behavior. When a customer leaves a high rating, it signals satisfaction that predicts whether they will return. Internal operational metrics like inventory levels, workforce productivity, and sales forecast accuracy do not have a direct causal relationship with how a customer rates their experience. A customer has no idea whether inventory was optimally managed that week — they only know whether the experience was good enough to bring them back.

Assessment 2 confusion: "Inventory level" was selected. Customers cannot see inventory levels and do not factor them into ratings. The correlation runs between satisfaction, rating, and future purchasing behavior.
Process Design & Product Development
Process types — flow shop, job shop, project, and continuous flow
Missed — Both Assessments

A flow shop uses sequential workstations where products move through the same steps in the same order. Multiple similar products — like different vehicle models — can all move through the same assembly line because the fundamental process sequence is shared. This is automobile manufacturing: different models share the assembly sequence even though specific parts differ.

A job shop handles custom, unique work where each job may follow a completely different sequence of operations. A project process is a one-time unique endeavor with a defined start and end — building a cruise ship, constructing a skyscraper. A continuous flow process runs 24/7 producing a single product, like an oil refinery or chemical plant, where stopping and restarting is more costly than never stopping.

Both assessments confusion: Automobile assembly of multiple models was selected as a job shop. Job shops handle one-off custom work in varying sequences. Auto assembly is repetitive and sequential — every car goes through the same stations in the same order. That is a flow shop.
House of quality, QFD, and competitive evaluation
Missed — Both Assessments

Quality Function Deployment (QFD) translates customer needs into engineering requirements using the House of Quality matrix. The most important input to QFD is the Voice of the Customer (VOC) — the direct expression of customer needs and wants. Everything else in the House of Quality flows from it. The House of Quality is the tool, the output, the framework — it cannot be its own input.

Within the matrix, the competitive evaluation section compares your product against competitors on each customer requirement. This is the section directly tied to differentiation — it reveals where your product is stronger or weaker in the marketplace relative to rivals.

Quick structure: Left = Voice of Customer (inputs). Top = Technical features (how product delivers). Center = Relationship matrix (which features satisfy which needs). Right = Competitive evaluation (comparison to rivals).
Both assessments confusion on QFD input: The House of Quality was selected. The House of Quality is the entire diagram — you cannot put the output back in as the starting input. VOC is always the input.
Design philosophies — manufacturability, reliability, and assembly
Missed — Both Assessments

Design for Manufacturability (DFM) focuses on making a product easy and efficient to produce at the highest possible quality — reducing production costs, minimizing floor errors, and ensuring designs translate smoothly from drawings to physical reality.

Design for Reliability focuses on ensuring the product performs its intended function for a specified period under defined conditions. This is about functional longevity — a probability-based concept: given these conditions and this time window, what is the likelihood of failure-free performance?

Design for Assembly (DFA) focuses specifically on minimizing parts and simplifying how components fit together. It overlaps with DFM but is narrower — focused only on the assembly step.

Both assessments confusion: Design for Reliability was selected when the question described designing for efficient production at the highest quality. Reliability is about how long something keeps working — not how efficiently it is made. DFM spans both production efficiency and quality simultaneously.
3D printing — best business application
Missed — Assessment 1

3D printing's most powerful business application is prototyping before scaling production. Companies use it to quickly produce physical test models before investing in expensive tooling required for mass manufacturing. This compresses development timelines and reduces the cost of discovering design errors late in the process. 3D printing is too slow and expensive per unit for mass production — its value is concentrated in the early development phase where design iteration speed matters most.

Assessment 1 confusion: "Increasing manufacturing productivity" was selected. 3D printing does not improve the throughput of a mass production line — it is a development and prototyping tool, not a production scaling tool.
Supply Chain Design & Fulfillment
Postponement strategy in supply chain design
Missed — Both Assessments

Postponement delays final product customization or differentiation until the latest possible point — as close to actual customer demand as possible. Rather than producing finished, differentiated products far upstream based on uncertain forecasts, companies keep products in a generic state longer and complete customization regionally based on real demand. A company that ships bulk product to regional warehouses and packages it locally into different sizes is practicing postponement — the packaging decision is postponed to where actual demand is known.

This is commonly confused with vertical integration, which means a company owns more stages of its supply chain. Vertical integration is about ownership and control of supply chain stages. Postponement is about the timing of product differentiation. They are entirely different strategic concepts.

Both assessments confusion: Vertical integration was selected in both attempts. The scenario described is about when and where customization happens — that is postponement. Vertical integration is about acquiring or owning supply chain stages.
Make-to-stock vs make-to-order — the key distinction
Missed — Assessment 1

The single most important distinction between make-to-stock (MTS) and make-to-order (MTO) is whether the company holds finished goods inventory before demand arrives. In MTS, products are manufactured based on forecasts and held in stock ready to ship immediately. In MTO, production does not begin until a customer order is received — no finished goods inventory is held. Both strategies still require manufacturing and raw materials planning. Only MTS requires pre-building and holding finished goods.

Assessment 1 confusion: "The need to manufacture the finished good" was selected. Both MTS and MTO manufacture finished goods — that is not a distinguishing factor. Only MTS requires holding finished goods inventory before the order arrives.
Fixed and variable cost math — make-or-buy decisions
Missed — Both Assessments

When evaluating in-house production versus outsourcing, total cost is calculated using a fixed-plus-variable model. Fixed costs do not change with production volume. Variable costs change proportionally with output.

Total Cost = Fixed Cost + (Variable Cost per Unit × Number of Units)

A $4,000 fixed cost plus a $5-per-unit variable cost for 1,000 units: $4,000 + ($5 × 1,000) = $4,000 + $5,000 = $9,000. Variable cost is per unit. 1,000 units × $5/unit = $5,000 — not $5,000,000. Always verify your unit of measure before multiplying, and check that the result passes a basic reasonableness check.

Both assessments confusion: $4,005,000 was selected in both attempts — treating $5 as if applied to one million units rather than one thousand. A unit-of-measure error inflated the answer by a factor of 1,000.
Supply chain risk types — economic vs logistics vs infrastructure vs capacity
Missed — Both Assessments

Supply chain risks are categorized by what caused the disruption, not what was disrupted. Economic risk arises from financial, regulatory, or trade policy changes — tariffs, new taxes, currency shifts, sanctions. Logistics risk involves physical disruptions to the movement of goods — port strikes, trucking shortages, vessel groundings. Infrastructure risk involves failures of physical systems like roads, bridges, or power grids. Capacity risk is the inability to produce enough to meet demand.

When a shipment is halted at a border because a government changed its tax structure, no truck broke down and no port backed up — a policy changed. That is economic risk.

Both assessments confusion: Logistics risk was selected for a tax-regime delay. Logistics risk applies when physical transportation mechanisms fail. A policy change causing the delay is economic risk. Always ask: what caused the disruption, not just where it happened.
Backhaul and consolidation in logistics
Missed — Assessment 1

Backhaul refers to using the return leg of a delivery route to carry freight rather than driving empty. When a truck delivers from A to B, it must return to A. Rather than making that trip empty, backhaul arranges return freight, converting wasted capacity into a revenue-generating move. Consolidation, by contrast, means combining multiple small shipments from different shippers into a single truckload going in one direction. Consolidation aggregates cargo going the same way. Backhaul productively uses the return trip of a vehicle that has already completed its delivery.

Assessment 1 confusion: Consolidation was selected. The truck in the scenario had already completed its delivery and was carrying new cargo on the return trip — that is backhaul, not combining loads from multiple shippers.
Bullwhip effect — demand amplification in supply chains
Missed — Assessment 1

The bullwhip effect describes how small fluctuations in consumer demand become progressively amplified as the demand signal travels upstream through retailers, wholesalers, distributors, and manufacturers — because each party adds its own safety buffer on top of what it already received. A modest consumer uptick might cause a retailer to order 15% more, the wholesaler to order 25% more, and by the time the signal reaches the manufacturer, it looks like a massive demand surge requiring emergency production ramp-up. The name comes from cracking a whip: a small wrist flick creates a large wave at the tip.

Demand variance is different — it is normal statistical fluctuation in demand. The bullwhip effect is the artificial amplification of that variance caused by independent buffering at every chain level.

Assessment 1 confusion: Demand variance was selected. Demand variance is the normal statistical fluctuation. Bullwhip is what happens when each supply chain member independently buffers against uncertainty, distorting and amplifying the variance as it moves upstream.
Multisourcing — when to use multiple suppliers and when not to
Missed — Both Assessments

Multisourcing means using multiple suppliers for the same component. It makes the most sense when a component requires supply flexibility and carries high supply risk. Multiple suppliers protect against disruption — if one source fails, production continues from others. Multiple sources also allow volume shifting when demand or conditions change.

Multisourcing does not make sense for IP-intensive components because production knowledge shared across multiple suppliers risks exposing proprietary information. It also tends not to work for high-capital-investment components because dividing volume across multiple suppliers means none achieves the economies of scale a single consolidated supplier would produce.

Both assessments confusion: IP-intensive or technologically complex components were selected as appropriate for multisourcing. In reality those components benefit from single-source relationships where proprietary knowledge stays protected and the supplier relationship is deep and specialized.
Process Analysis & Capacity
Station utilization — calculating correctly when multiple workers are involved
Missed — Both Assessments

Utilization measures what fraction of a resource's maximum capacity is actually being used.

Utilization = Demand Rate ÷ Capacity Rate

The most common error in multi-worker scenarios is forgetting to add up all the capacity at the station before dividing. If two workers each process 50 units per hour, the station's combined capacity is 100 units per hour. If 60 units per hour flow to the station, utilization = 60 ÷ 100 = 60%. The station has surplus capacity. A utilization above 100% signals a bottleneck.

Both assessments confusion: 83% was selected in both attempts — likely from dividing 60 by a single inspector's capacity of 50. There are two inspectors, making total capacity 100. Always sum all parallel capacity at a station before calculating utilization.
Throughput in serial processes — the bottleneck determines everything
Missed — Assessment 2

In a serial process where each step feeds directly into the next, overall throughput is determined entirely by the slowest step — the bottleneck. No matter how fast other steps operate, the system cannot produce faster than the bottleneck allows. Everything upstream builds a queue; everything downstream is perpetually starved. This is the foundation of the Theory of Constraints: improving any step other than the bottleneck produces no improvement in system throughput.

Visual shortcut: Water flowing through four pipes connected in series — the narrowest pipe determines system flow regardless of how wide the others are.
Assessment 2 confusion: 85 units/hr was selected — the second-lowest rate, not the lowest. The bottleneck is always the single minimum output rate in the chain. Near-minimum does not count; only the actual minimum constrains the system.
EOQ formula — why doubling demand doesn't double the order quantity
Missed — Both Assessments

The Economic Order Quantity formula balances ordering costs against holding costs. Because demand appears inside a square root, changes in demand have a dampened effect on the resulting order quantity.

EOQ = √(2 × D × S ÷ H)

When demand doubles, new EOQ = √2 × original EOQ. √2 ≈ 1.414, meaning EOQ increases by about 41% — roughly 40%. The square root dampens the effect because as demand increases you order more per order, but you also reorder more frequently, so individual order size grows more slowly than demand itself.

Both assessments confusion: A 200% increase was selected in both attempts — which would mean tripling the EOQ. That ignores the square root entirely. Whenever EOQ changes in response to demand, remember the square root cuts the proportional effect roughly in half.
WIP inventory and cycle inventory — two distinct inventory concepts
Missed — Assessment 1

Work-in-Process (WIP) inventory consists of partially completed goods somewhere in the middle of a production process — past raw materials, not yet finished goods. Its primary purpose is to buffer between sequential process activities, absorbing timing mismatches so a slowdown at one step does not immediately halt the next.

Cycle inventory is the average inventory held because ordering happens in batches. Because companies order in lots — say 500 units at a time — inventory cycles between a high point right after delivery and near-zero before the next order. The average is half the lot size.

Average Cycle Inventory = Lot Size ÷ 2
Assessment 1 confusion: On WIP, "meets expected customer demand" was selected — that describes finished goods inventory. On cycle inventory, the answer "2" was selected when lot size was 500. The number 2 is the divisor in the formula, not the result. 500 ÷ 2 = 250.
Forecasting Methods
Forecasting techniques — SMA, multiple regression, Delphi, and exponential smoothing
Missed — Assessment 2Partial — Assessment 1

The simple moving average (SMA) averages actual demand from the most recent N periods. Three months of X units each: (X+X+X) ÷ 3 = X. The average of three identical values is that same value — not the sum (3X). Always divide by the number of periods.

Multiple linear regression is the correct technique when forecasting involves more than one independent variable simultaneously. The Delphi method is qualitative and expert-consensus based — no formula, no variables. These are very different: one is quantitative and data-driven, the other is qualitative and judgment-driven.

In regression, the required parameter is the slope (b in Forecast = a + bX). "Gradient" is a calculus term for a related concept but is not standard operations management terminology.

In simple exponential smoothing, a low smoothing constant (α = 0.2) gives heavy weight to historical data — low responsiveness to recent changes. A high α (0.8) reacts strongly to the most recent actual demand — high responsiveness.

Assessment 2 confusion: Delphi method was selected for a multi-variable forecasting scenario. Delphi is qualitative and handles no mathematical variable relationships. Multiple linear regression is the only quantitative technique designed for multiple independent variables simultaneously.
Sustainability, Ethics & Governance
ISO standards — four standards, four completely different scopes
Missed — Both Assessments

ISO 9001 governs quality management systems — consistent products and services meeting customer requirements. ISO 14000 governs environmental management systems — frameworks for managing and reducing environmental impact cost-effectively. ISO 26000 provides guidance on social responsibility covering the full ESG framework: environmental, social, and governance dimensions together. ISO 27001 governs information security management — protecting data and managing cybersecurity risk.

Memory anchor: ISO 9001 = Quality (9 → Q → Quality). ISO 14000 = Environment (14 → forest → nature). ISO 26000 = Society/ESG (broader social scope). ISO 27001 = Security (27 → 007 → James Bond → security). When a question references the full ESG framework, the answer is ISO 26000 — not ISO 14000, which covers only the environmental pillar.
Both assessments confusion: Assessment 1 picked ISO 27001 for ESG guidance. Assessment 2 picked ISO 14000. ISO 14000 covers only environmental management — one ESG pillar. ISO 26000 addresses all three ESG pillars together.
Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions — where in the value chain emissions originate
Missed — Both Assessments

Scope 1 = direct emissions from company-owned sources: your factory's smokestacks, your company vehicle fleet, your on-site generators. Scope 2 = indirect emissions from purchased electricity, steam, heating, or cooling. Scope 3 = all other indirect emissions across the entire value chain — upstream from suppliers and downstream from customers. Scope 3 is typically the largest category for most companies and the hardest to measure.

To reduce Scope 3 emissions, a company must act on external parties — primarily suppliers. Setting emission reduction targets for key suppliers, including sustainability requirements in procurement contracts, or shifting to lower-emission suppliers. No change inside the company's own walls reduces Scope 3; those internal changes only affect Scope 1.

Both assessments confusion: Controlling the company's own manufacturing plant emissions was selected as the Scope 3 reduction method. Manufacturing plant emissions are Scope 1 — direct and owned. Scope 3 requires acting on suppliers and value chain partners, not on internal operations.
Corporate governance — structure, ratings, and what they actually measure
Missed — Both Assessments

Corporate governance is the system of rules and processes defining how investors (shareholders) oversee management and how authority is divided between owners and the managers who act on their behalf. It is distinct from corporate social responsibility (voluntary ethical behavior toward communities) and from the triple bottom line (a multi-dimensional measurement framework).

Corporate governance rating systems evaluate how effectively the board of directors represents shareholder interests. The key factors are the quality and diversity of directors — independence from management, breadth of expertise, and diversity of backgrounds. A board dominated by insiders scores poorly. A board with independent directors from varied professional backgrounds scores well. Product quality, supplier quality, and distribution reliability are operational metrics — not governance metrics.

Both assessments confusion: "Quality and consistency of products and services" was selected in both attempts. That is an operational metric. Corporate governance ratings assess the people overseeing the company — board composition — not the products the company makes.
Business ethics eras — Industrial Revolution vs mercantilism
Missed — Assessment 2

The Industrial Revolution and post-industrial era (mid-1700s through early 1900s) is defined by singular focus on maximizing shareholder profit with minimal concern for workers, communities, or environment. The rise of factories, mass production, and publicly traded corporations created structures where shareholder returns were treated as the sole legitimate purpose of a business.

The mercantilism era predates industrialization. It describes a period when nations — not individual corporations — competed for wealth through trade surpluses, colonization, and accumulation of precious metals. The actors are different (nation-states vs corporations) and the mechanism is different (trade policy vs shareholder capitalism).

Assessment 2 confusion: Mercantilism was selected. Mercantilism is about national trade policy before the shareholder corporation existed. The era defined by shareholder primacy and profit maximization with little stakeholder concern is the Industrial Revolution and post-industrial era.
Competing values in new market entry — economic development vs environmental protection
Missed — Both Assessments

When an industrial company establishes operations in a new country, it faces a fundamental tension between two genuinely positive but competing goals. Economic development: the operation creates jobs, generates tax revenue, builds infrastructure, and stimulates local economies. Environmental protection: the operations may damage ecosystems, contaminate water sources, and create long-term environmental harm. These two values directly compete — aggressively pursuing one often directly undermines the other.

Both assessments confusion: "Ethical decision-making and long-term sustainability" was selected. That describes how to approach the dilemma — the methodology — not what the competing values themselves are. The values in tension are specifically economic development and environmental protection.
Triple bottom line — measuring the full scope of corporate social responsibility
Missed — Assessment 1

The triple bottom line (TBL) measures corporate performance across three dimensions simultaneously: People (social impact), Planet (environmental impact), and Profit (economic performance). Traditional financial reporting captures only profit. The TBL adds social and environmental performance as equally important measures of long-term corporate health. A code of conduct sets behavioral rules but does not measure outcomes. A balance sheet captures financial position only. An audit report verifies compliance. None of these captures all three CSR dimensions the way the TBL does.

Assessment 1 confusion: Code of conduct was selected. A code of conduct tells people how to behave — it does not measure how well a company is actually performing across social, environmental, and economic dimensions.
Quality & Continuous Improvement
Six Sigma — understanding DPMO and what the standard actually requires
Missed — Both Assessments

Six Sigma defines near-perfect process performance as no more than 3.4 defects per million opportunities (DPMO). To determine how many units must be produced for a given defect count to represent Six Sigma quality, divide the defect count by 3.4 and multiply by one million. Fifty-one defects: 51 ÷ 3.4 = 15 million units required. At only 1 million units, 51 defects would equal 51 DPMO — fifteen times worse than Six Sigma quality.

Both assessments confusion: 1 million units was selected in both attempts. That answer treats the absolute defect count as if it were the DPMO itself. 51 defects out of 1 million units = 51 DPMO, not Six Sigma quality. The correct minimum volume is 15 million units.
Lean waste types — motion vs waiting vs over-processing
Missed — Assessment 2

Motion waste is unnecessary physical movement by workers — walking farther than necessary, reaching awkwardly, searching for tools that should be at hand. The cause is typically poor workplace layout. Waiting waste is idle time — a worker or machine stopped because something upstream hasn't arrived yet. The worker is stationary and unproductive due to a timing mismatch. Over-processing waste is doing more work than the customer actually requires — extra finish coats, redundant quality checks, using precision equipment for imprecise work.

Assessment 2 confusion: Over-processing was selected for a scenario where workers walk to an inconvenient storage area. That unnecessary walking is motion waste — the worker's body is moving unnecessarily due to poor layout. Over-processing means doing more steps than required, which is a different problem entirely.
Pull systems — matching production exactly to real demand
Missed — Both Assessments

A pull system triggers production only in response to actual customer demand — a customer order, a kanban signal, an inventory replenishment trigger. Nothing is produced until demand authorizes it. Pull systems directly solve overproduction — one of Lean's eight wastes. When production consistently exceeds actual demand, a pull system ties production rate to actual consumption, eliminating unwanted inventory buildup.

A push system schedules production based on demand forecasts and runs regardless of whether output has been requested. Push systems cause overproduction when forecasts are inaccurate. Six Sigma reduces defects, not excess volume. EOQ is an order-sizing formula, not a production control philosophy.

Both assessments confusion: Assessment 1 selected Six Sigma; Assessment 2 selected push system — for an overproduction problem. Six Sigma reduces defects, not volume. A push system is the cause of overproduction, not the solution. The remedy is a pull system that ties production to actual demand.
Disruptive vs sustaining innovation
Missed — Assessment 2

Disruptive innovation creates an entirely new market or fundamentally transforms how an existing industry operates — something so novel it changes the rules of competition. A first-of-its-kind digital platform for supply chain risk prediction is disruptive: nothing like it previously existed, and it changes how supply chain professionals manage risk entirely. Upgrading existing software is sustaining innovation — it improves what already exists without changing market structure. A new product variant is incremental innovation. Launching a product a competitor already offers is imitation. None of these qualify as disruptive.

Assessment 2 confusion: Introducing a new mountain bike variant was selected. A new variant of an existing product in an established category is incremental product development — nothing disruptive about adding another configuration to an existing product line.
