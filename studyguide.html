Operations & Competitive Strategy
Flexibility as a competitive priority
Missed — Assessment 1

In operations management, companies compete along four main priorities: cost, quality, time, and flexibility. Flexibility is the competitive priority that captures a company's ability to adapt quickly to changing customer requirements — whether that means adjusting product designs, shifting production volumes, or handling unique requests on short notice.

Service quality measures how well a service is delivered. Customer satisfaction is an outcome — a result of performance, not a capability metric. Processing time measures speed and efficiency. Flexibility specifically answers: how easily can this organization change when requirements shift? A manufacturer that can retool its line from one product to another in hours has high flexibility. A company locked into a single configuration has low flexibility regardless of how precise or fast its work is.

Assessment 1 confusion: "Service quality measure" was selected. Service quality tells you how well something was done — not how adaptable the operation is. These are fundamentally different performance dimensions.
Customer benefit packages — core vs peripheral goods
Missed — Both Assessments

A customer benefit package (CBP) is the complete bundle of goods and services a customer receives. At the center is the core product. Surrounding it are peripheral items that add value and differentiate the seller. The critical distinction is between peripheral goods (tangible, physically separate items) and peripheral services (intangible support activities).

Financing options and online customer support are services — intangible and not physically part of the package. Software preloaded on a device is part of the core product experience — embedded in what you are buying, not added on top. A physical accessory like a replacement battery or charger is a true peripheral good: tangible, physically separate, and its inclusion creates a differentiated offer a competitor might not match.

Both assessments confusion: Preloaded software was selected. Because it ships as part of the core functionality, it is not a peripheral good. Peripheral goods must be physically separate items that add value beyond the main product.
Customer ratings and future purchase behavior
Missed — Assessment 2

Customer ratings are most strongly correlated with future purchase behavior. When a customer leaves a high rating, it signals satisfaction that predicts whether they will return. Internal operational metrics like inventory levels, workforce productivity, and forecast accuracy do not have a direct causal relationship with how a customer rates their experience. A customer has no idea whether inventory was optimally managed — they only know whether the experience was good enough to bring them back.

Assessment 2 confusion: "Inventory level" was selected. Customers cannot see inventory levels and do not factor them into ratings. The correlation runs between satisfaction, rating, and future purchasing behavior.
Process Design & Product Development
Process types — flow shop, job shop, project, and continuous flow
Missed — Both Assessments

A flow shop uses sequential workstations where products move through the same steps in the same order. Multiple similar products — like different vehicle models — share the assembly sequence even though specific parts differ. This is automobile manufacturing.

A job shop handles custom, unique work where each job may follow a completely different sequence of operations. A project process is a one-time unique endeavor — building a cruise ship, constructing a skyscraper. A continuous flow process runs 24/7 producing a single product, like an oil refinery, where stopping and restarting is more costly than never stopping.

Both assessments confusion: Automobile assembly was selected as a job shop. Job shops handle one-off custom work in varying sequences. Auto assembly is repetitive and sequential — every car goes through the same stations in the same order. That is a flow shop.
House of quality, QFD, and competitive evaluation
Missed — Both Assessments

Quality Function Deployment (QFD) translates customer needs into engineering requirements. The most important input to QFD is the Voice of the Customer (VOC) — the direct expression of customer needs. The House of Quality is the tool, the output, the framework — it cannot be its own input.

Within the matrix, the competitive evaluation section compares your product against competitors on each customer requirement — directly tied to differentiation strategy.

Quick structure: Left = VOC (inputs). Top = Technical features (how the product delivers). Center = Relationship matrix. Right = Competitive evaluation (comparison to rivals).
Both assessments confusion on QFD input: The House of Quality was selected. It is the diagram you build from the VOC — you cannot put the output back in as the starting input.
Design philosophies — manufacturability, reliability, and assembly
Missed — Both Assessments

Design for Manufacturability (DFM) makes a product easy and efficient to produce at the highest possible quality — reducing production costs and minimizing floor errors.

Design for Reliability ensures the product performs its intended function for a specified period under defined conditions. This is about functional longevity — a probability-based concept: given these conditions and this time window, what is the likelihood of failure-free performance?

Design for Assembly (DFA) focuses specifically on minimizing parts and simplifying assembly steps. It overlaps with DFM but is narrower — focused only on the assembly phase.

Both assessments confusion: Design for Reliability was selected when the question described designing for efficient production at the highest quality. Reliability is about how long something keeps working — not how efficiently it is made. DFM spans both production efficiency and quality.
3D printing — best business application
Missed — Assessment 1

3D printing's most powerful business application is prototyping before scaling production. Companies use it to quickly produce physical test models before investing in expensive tooling required for mass manufacturing. It is too slow and expensive per unit for mass production — its value is concentrated in the early development phase where design iteration speed matters most.

Assessment 1 confusion: "Increasing manufacturing productivity" was selected. 3D printing does not improve throughput of a mass production line — it is a prototyping and development tool.
Supply Chain Design & Fulfillment
Postponement strategy in supply chain design
Missed — Both Assessments

Postponement delays final product customization until the latest possible point — as close to actual customer demand as possible. A company that ships bulk product to regional warehouses and packages it locally into different sizes is practicing postponement — the packaging decision is postponed to where actual demand is known.

This is commonly confused with vertical integration, which means a company owns more stages of its supply chain. Vertical integration is about ownership. Postponement is about the timing of differentiation. They are entirely different concepts.

Both assessments confusion: Vertical integration was selected in both attempts. The scenario is about when and where customization happens — that is postponement.
Make-to-stock vs make-to-order — the key distinction
Missed — Assessment 1

The single most important distinction is whether the company holds finished goods inventory before demand arrives. In MTS, products are manufactured based on forecasts and held in stock. In MTO, production does not begin until a customer order is received — no finished goods inventory is held. Both strategies still require manufacturing and materials planning. Only MTS requires pre-building finished goods.

Assessment 1 confusion: "The need to manufacture the finished good" was selected. Both MTS and MTO manufacture finished goods — that is not a distinguishing factor. Only MTS requires holding finished goods inventory before the order arrives.
Fixed and variable cost math — make-or-buy decisions
Missed — Both Assessments

Fixed costs do not change with production volume. Variable costs change proportionally with output.

Total Cost = Fixed Cost + (Variable Cost per Unit × Number of Units)

$4,000 fixed + $5/unit variable × 1,000 units = $4,000 + $5,000 = $9,000. Variable cost is per unit. 1,000 × $5 = $5,000 — not $5,000,000. Always check your unit of measure.

Both assessments confusion: $4,005,000 was selected — treating $5 as if applied to one million units rather than one thousand. A unit-of-measure error inflated the answer by a factor of 1,000.
Supply chain risk types — economic vs logistics vs infrastructure vs capacity
Missed — Both Assessments

Categorize by what caused the disruption, not what was disrupted. Economic risk = financial, regulatory, or trade policy changes (tariffs, taxes, sanctions). Logistics risk = physical disruptions to movement (port strikes, trucking shortages). Infrastructure risk = physical system failures (roads, bridges, power grids). Capacity risk = inability to produce enough.

When a shipment is halted at a border because a government changed its tax structure — no truck broke down, no port backed up — a policy changed. That is economic risk.

Both assessments confusion: Logistics risk was selected for a tax-regime delay. Logistics risk applies when physical transportation mechanisms fail. A policy change is economic risk. Always ask: what caused the disruption?
Backhaul and consolidation in logistics
Missed — Assessment 1

Backhaul = using the return leg of a delivery route to carry freight rather than driving empty. Consolidation = combining multiple small shipments from different shippers into a single truckload going in one direction. Consolidation aggregates cargo going the same way. Backhaul productively uses the return trip of a vehicle that already completed its delivery.

Assessment 1 confusion: Consolidation was selected. The truck in the scenario had already completed its delivery and was carrying new cargo on the return trip — that is backhaul.
Bullwhip effect — demand amplification in supply chains
Missed — Assessment 1

The bullwhip effect describes how small fluctuations in consumer demand become progressively amplified upstream — because each supply chain member adds its own safety buffer on top of what they received. A modest consumer uptick causes the retailer to order 15% more, the wholesaler 25% more, and by the manufacturer the signal looks like a massive surge. The name comes from cracking a whip: a small wrist flick creates a large wave at the tip.

Demand variance is different — it is normal statistical fluctuation. The bullwhip effect is the artificial amplification caused by independent buffering at every level.

Assessment 1 confusion: Demand variance was selected. Bullwhip is specifically the amplification phenomenon — not the underlying variance itself.
Multisourcing — when it helps and when it doesn't
Missed — Both Assessments

Multisourcing makes the most sense when a component requires supply flexibility and carries high supply risk. Multiple suppliers protect against disruption — if one source fails, production continues. Multisourcing does not make sense for IP-intensive components (risks exposing proprietary information) or high-capital-investment components (divides volume, preventing economies of scale).

Both assessments confusion: IP-intensive components were selected as appropriate for multisourcing. In reality those benefit from single-source relationships where proprietary knowledge stays protected.
Process Analysis & Capacity
Station utilization — calculating correctly when multiple workers are involved
Missed — Both Assessments
Utilization = Demand Rate ÷ Capacity Rate

If two workers each process 50 units/hr, combined station capacity = 100 units/hr. If 60 units/hr flow to the station, utilization = 60 ÷ 100 = 60%. A utilization above 100% signals a bottleneck.

Both assessments confusion: 83% was selected — from dividing 60 by a single inspector's capacity of 50. There are two inspectors, making total capacity 100. Always sum all parallel capacity at a station before dividing.
Throughput in serial processes — the bottleneck determines everything
Missed — Assessment 2

In a serial process, overall throughput = the bottleneck = the slowest step. No matter how fast other steps operate, the system cannot produce faster than the bottleneck allows. Everything upstream builds a queue; everything downstream is starved. In a four-step process with outputs of 280, 60, 45, and 85 units/hr, throughput = 45 units/hr.

Visual: Water through four pipes in series — the narrowest pipe determines system flow regardless of how wide the others are.
Assessment 2 confusion: 85 units/hr was selected — the second-lowest rate. The bottleneck is always the single minimum output rate. Near-minimum does not count.
EOQ formula — why doubling demand doesn't double the order quantity
Missed — Both Assessments
EOQ = √(2 × D × S ÷ H)

When demand doubles: new EOQ = √2 × original EOQ. √2 ≈ 1.414 → about 41% increase ≈ 40%. The square root dampens the effect. As demand increases you order more per order, but also reorder more frequently, so individual order size grows more slowly than demand.

Both assessments confusion: 200% increase was selected — which would mean tripling EOQ. That ignores the square root entirely. The square root cuts the proportional effect roughly in half.
WIP inventory and cycle inventory — two distinct concepts
Missed — Assessment 1

Work-in-Process (WIP) inventory = partially completed goods in the middle of production. Its purpose is to buffer between sequential process activities, absorbing timing mismatches. Cycle inventory = average inventory held because ordering happens in batches.

Average Cycle Inventory = Lot Size ÷ 2

If lot size = 500 units, average cycle inventory = 250 units.

Assessment 1 confusion: WIP was said to "meet expected customer demand" — that describes finished goods. On cycle inventory, the answer "2" was selected (the divisor, not the result). 500 ÷ 2 = 250.
Forecasting Methods
Forecasting techniques — SMA, multiple regression, Delphi, and exponential smoothing
Missed — Assessment 2Partial — Assessment 1

The SMA averages demand from the most recent N periods. Three months of X units each: (X+X+X) ÷ 3 = X. Always divide by the number of periods — the result is the average, not the sum.

Multiple linear regression = forecasting with more than one independent variable simultaneously. The Delphi method is qualitative and expert-consensus based — no formula, no variables. The required parameter in regression is the slope (b in Forecast = a + bX). "Gradient" is a calculus term — not standard OM terminology.

In exponential smoothing, low α (e.g. 0.2) = heavy weight on history, low responsiveness. High α (e.g. 0.8) = high responsiveness to recent demand changes.

Assessment 2 confusion: Delphi was selected for a multi-variable forecasting scenario. Delphi is qualitative with no mathematical variable relationships. Multiple linear regression is the quantitative technique for multiple independent variables.
Sustainability, Ethics & Governance
ISO standards — four standards, four completely different scopes
Missed — Both Assessments

ISO 9001 = quality management systems. ISO 14000 = environmental management systems. ISO 26000 = social responsibility / full ESG framework (environmental + social + governance). ISO 27001 = information security management.

Memory anchor: ISO 9001 = Quality (Q). ISO 14000 = Environment (forest/nature). ISO 26000 = Society/ESG (broadest scope). ISO 27001 = Security (007 → James Bond → security). Full ESG framework = ISO 26000, not ISO 14000 which covers only the environmental pillar.
Both assessments confusion: Assessment 1 picked ISO 27001 for ESG. Assessment 2 picked ISO 14000. ISO 14000 is only one ESG pillar. ISO 26000 covers all three.
Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions
Missed — Both Assessments

Scope 1 = direct emissions from company-owned sources (your factory, your fleet, your generators). Scope 2 = indirect emissions from purchased electricity, steam, heating, or cooling. Scope 3 = all other indirect emissions across the entire value chain — from suppliers upstream and customers downstream.

To reduce Scope 3, you must act on external parties — setting emission targets for key suppliers, adjusting procurement requirements, or shifting to lower-emission suppliers. Internal changes only affect Scope 1.

Both assessments confusion: Controlling the company's own manufacturing plant emissions was selected as the Scope 3 reduction method. Manufacturing plant emissions = Scope 1. Scope 3 requires acting on suppliers and value chain partners.
Corporate governance — structure, ratings, and what they actually measure
Missed — Both Assessments

Corporate governance = the system defining how shareholders oversee management and how authority is divided between owners and the managers who act on their behalf. Distinct from CSR (voluntary ethical behavior) and the triple bottom line (a measurement framework).

Governance rating systems evaluate the quality and diversity of directors — independence from management, breadth of expertise, and diversity of backgrounds. Product quality, supplier quality, and distribution reliability are operational metrics — not governance metrics.

Both assessments confusion: "Quality and consistency of products and services" was selected. That is an operational metric. Governance ratings assess who oversees the company — board composition — not what the company produces.
Business ethics eras — Industrial Revolution vs mercantilism
Missed — Assessment 2

The Industrial Revolution and post-industrial era (mid-1700s onward) = singular focus on maximizing shareholder profit with minimal concern for workers, communities, or environment. The rise of factories and publicly traded corporations made shareholder returns the sole purpose of a business.

The mercantilism era predates industrialization. It describes nation-states — not corporations — competing for wealth through trade surpluses and colonization. Different actors (nation-states vs corporations) and different mechanism (trade policy vs shareholder capitalism).

Assessment 2 confusion: Mercantilism was selected. Mercantilism is about national trade policy before the shareholder corporation existed. The era defined by shareholder primacy is the Industrial Revolution and post-industrial era.
Competing values in new market entry
Missed — Both Assessments

When an industrial company establishes operations in a new country, the fundamental tension is between economic development (jobs, tax revenue, infrastructure) and environmental protection (ecosystem damage, water contamination, long-term harm). These two legitimately positive goals directly compete — aggressively pursuing one often directly undermines the other.

Both assessments confusion: "Ethical decision-making and sustainability" was selected. That describes the methodology for approaching the dilemma, not the competing values themselves. The values in tension are economic development and environmental protection.
Triple bottom line — measuring CSR success
Missed — Assessment 1

The triple bottom line (TBL) measures corporate performance across three dimensions: People (social impact), Planet (environmental impact), and Profit (economic performance). Traditional financial reporting captures only profit. A code of conduct sets behavioral rules — it does not measure outcomes. A balance sheet captures financial position only. Only the TBL captures all three CSR dimensions together.

Assessment 1 confusion: Code of conduct was selected. A code of conduct tells people how to behave — it does not measure performance across social, environmental, and economic dimensions.
Quality & Continuous Improvement
Six Sigma — understanding DPMO and minimum production volume
Missed — Both Assessments

Six Sigma = no more than 3.4 defects per million opportunities (DPMO). To find the minimum production volume for a given defect count to represent Six Sigma quality: divide the defect count by 3.4 and multiply by one million.

51 defects: 51 ÷ 3.4 = 15 million units required. At 1 million units, 51 defects = 51 DPMO — fifteen times worse than Six Sigma quality.

Both assessments confusion: 1 million units was selected. That treats the absolute defect count as if it were the DPMO itself. 51 defects ÷ 3.4 DPMO = 15 million units needed.
Lean waste types — motion vs waiting vs over-processing
Missed — Assessment 2

Motion waste = unnecessary physical movement by workers — walking farther than necessary, searching for tools, reaching awkwardly. Caused by poor workplace layout. Waiting waste = idle time because something upstream hasn't arrived. Over-processing waste = doing more work than the customer requires — extra finish coats, redundant quality checks.

Assessment 2 confusion: Over-processing was selected for workers walking to an inconvenient storage area. That unnecessary walking is motion waste — the worker's body moves unnecessarily due to poor layout. Over-processing means doing more steps than required.
Pull systems — matching production to real demand
Missed — Both Assessments

A pull system triggers production only in response to actual customer demand — a customer order, a kanban signal, an inventory replenishment trigger. Nothing is produced until demand authorizes it. Pull systems directly solve overproduction. A push system schedules production based on demand forecasts and runs regardless of whether output has been requested — the cause of overproduction, not the solution. Six Sigma reduces defects, not excess volume. EOQ is an order-sizing formula, not a production control philosophy.

Both assessments confusion: Assessment 1 selected Six Sigma; Assessment 2 selected push system — for an overproduction problem. Six Sigma reduces defects, not volume. Push system causes overproduction. Pull system solves it.
Disruptive vs sustaining innovation
Missed — Assessment 2

Disruptive innovation creates an entirely new market or fundamentally transforms how an existing industry operates. A first-of-its-kind digital platform for supply chain risk prediction is disruptive — it creates a new category of tool and changes how professionals manage risk. Upgrading existing software = sustaining innovation. A new product variant = incremental innovation. Launching a competitor's existing product = imitation. Disruption changes the rules of competition, not just the product.

Assessment 2 confusion: Introducing a new mountain bike variant was selected. A new variant in an established category is incremental product development — nothing disruptive about adding another configuration to an existing product line.
