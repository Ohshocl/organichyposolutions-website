Q26 — What creates a monopoly?
MISSEDLegal
❌ You chose
Vertically integrated organizations
✅ Correct
Control of natural resources

The exam has one specific answer for monopoly creation: control of natural resources. If a company owns the only viable source of a critical input — a mineral deposit, an oil field, a rare earth mine — competitors simply cannot operate without that input, so the resource owner controls the entire market.

Vertical integration is a strategy (controlling multiple steps of a supply chain), but it doesn't inherently create a monopoly — competitors can still vertically integrate too. High interest rates and educated employees also don't block competitors from entering a market.

Rule: Monopoly = control of natural resources. The others (vertical integration, interest rates, employees) do not block competition in the same absolute way.
Q27 — Gap fillers for international contracts: CISG not UCC
MISSEDLegal
❌ You chose
Uniform Commercial Code
✅ Correct
Contracts for the International Sale of Goods (CISG)

Both UCC and CISG fill unstated terms in commercial contracts — but jurisdiction determines which applies. The UCC governs domestic U.S. contracts only. When two companies from different countries sign a contract and leave terms unstated, the CISG automatically applies as the default international gap-filler.

The question specified "two companies" (implying different countries) — that's the CISG signal. Any time you see a cross-border, multi-country contract with missing terms, the answer is CISG.

Rule: Two countries = CISG. One country (U.S. domestic) = UCC. The CISG is the international UCC equivalent.

Q41 — Distributor in a new market = Exporting, not Joint Venture
MISSEDEntry Strategy

✅ Correct
Exporting

The company hires a distributor to manage distribution in the new market. This is indirect exporting — the company still manufactures at home and ships internationally; it just uses a local intermediary for distribution. No joint entity is created, no brand rights are transferred, no manufacturing moves overseas.

A joint venture requires creating a new shared legal entity with a partner. That's not happening here — a hired distributor is just a sales channel, not a business partner with equity stakes.

Rule: Distributor + still ships from home = Exporting (indirect). The distribution mechanism doesn't change the entry strategy classification.

Decentralized manufacturing

The problem is physical: weather causes shipping delays, cutting profits. The solution must eliminate the shipping dependency. Decentralized manufacturing = building a production facility in (or near) the overseas market, so shipping becomes irrelevant. No more cross-ocean delays.

Market growth is a business goal, not a solution to a logistics problem. Flexible sourcing changes suppliers, not location. Economies of scale require consolidating production, not spreading it out.

Rule: Shipping delays = decentralized manufacturing (move production closer to market). Not market growth — that's a goal, not a logistics fix.
Q45 — Highest risk + slow entry + direct presence = Subsidiary (not JV)
MISSEDEntry Strategy
❌ You chose
Joint venture
✅ Correct
Subsidiary

The question specifies: direct operating presence + highest levels of risk + slow market entry. This maps precisely to a subsidiary (acquiring an existing company in the target country and operating it). The "highest risk" comes from taking full ownership; the "slow entry" refers to the acquisition and integration process.

A joint venture shares risk between two parties — it's high-risk but not the highest. The scenario also doesn't mention a local partner, which is the defining feature of a JV. Subsidiary = you own it entirely, you bear all the risk.

Note: If the question said "build from scratch, all assets, maximum risk" → that's Greenfield. This scenario says "buys an existing company" → Subsidiary.
Rule: Highest risk + direct presence + acquires existing = Subsidiary. JV = shared risk with a partner. Greenfield = builds from scratch.
Trade Theory — 3 misses
Q13 — Technology infrastructure → barriers to entry for other nations
MISSEDTrade
❌ You chose
It will improve supply and demand
✅ Correct
It will create barriers to entry for other nations

When a country has established technology infrastructure, other nations can't easily replicate it — it takes decades to build the knowledge base, equipment, and institutional capability. This is a genuine competitive moat that creates barriers to entry: competitors simply can't participate in that product's market without similar infrastructure.

"Improve supply and demand" is too generic — it doesn't describe what tech infrastructure specifically does. Economies of scale and natural resources are different competitive advantages entirely.

Rule: Established tech infrastructure = barriers to entry. Other countries can't compete without comparable capability, and building it takes time and massive investment.
Q16 — Quotas → domestic prices INCREASE (not decrease)
MISSEDTrade
❌ You chose
Decreases
✅ Correct
Increases

Basic supply and demand: a quota limits how much foreign supply can enter the market. Less supply + same consumer demand = higher prices. Domestic producers benefit from reduced competition and can charge more. Prices always increase when quotas restrict imports.

This is a cause/effect trap. The intuition "quota protects domestic goods → prices might go down" is backwards. Protection doesn't lower prices — it raises them by removing the price competition that imports provide.
Rule: Quota → less import supply → same demand → prices GO UP. Always. No exceptions.
Q21 — Drawback of regional trade agreements: shifts employment
MISSEDTrade
❌ You chose
They discourage trade with less efficient producers
✅ Correct
They shift employment opportunities

Regional trade agreements open borders to competition. When they do, production migrates toward the most cost-efficient location. Industries that can't compete lose jobs — the employment shifts to competitive sectors and away from non-competitive ones. This is structural unemployment, the documented main drawback.

"Discouraging trade with less efficient producers" is actually the opposite of what RTAs do — they promote trade, not discourage it. Cultural diversity is a benefit, not a drawback. Tariff protection ends when an RTA is formed.

Rule: RTA drawback = employment shift (structural unemployment in non-competitive sectors). Not discouraging trade — RTAs encourage trade.

Creation of isolationist policies

The question asks for the negative outcome on political systems. When globalization disrupts domestic industries and displaces workers, the political response is to turn inward — higher tariffs, immigration restrictions, withdrawal from trade agreements. This is the defining political backlash pattern: isolationist policies.

Increases in laws and taxes are generic governance outcomes unrelated to globalization specifically. Translation problems are operational, not political.

Q5 — Traditional economy + globalization = disadvantage (lower standard of living)
MISSEDGlobalization
❌ You chose

✅ Correct
At a disadvantage due to lower standard of living

A traditional economy has poor infrastructure and limited economic opportunity — both directly limit its ability to participate in global markets. The result is a disadvantage in globalization because its lower standard of living and infrastructure gaps prevent meaningful integration into global trade flows.

Globalization is not "unaffected" — the traditional economy is actively disadvantaged by it, not immune to it. Countries with more developed infrastructure and higher incomes participate more effectively in globalization.

Rule: Traditional economy = lower standard of living = disadvantaged in globalization. Not unaffected — disadvantaged.
Finance — 2 misses
Q61 — Reduce currency fluctuation effects → use a forward contract
MISSEDFinance
❌ You chose
Establish the price of a derivative
✅ Correct
Use a forward contract

A forward contract locks in an exchange rate today for a transaction that will occur on a specific future date. Both parties commit — neither can back out. This is the standard, direct answer to "how does a company protect itself from currency fluctuations over a defined future period?"

"Establish the price of a derivative" is a process step for pricing financial instruments generally — it's not an action a company takes to hedge currency risk. The action is using (executing) a forward contract, not establishing derivative pricing.

Rule: Hedge currency risk for a coming period = forward contract. Lock in today's rate for tomorrow's transaction. Not "establish derivative price" — that's a different function.
Q63 — Government nationalization of assets = micro risk (not macro)
MISSEDFinance
❌ You chose
Presence of nationalistic movements
✅ Correct
Government nationalization of assets (micro risk)

Macro risk affects ALL industries in a country simultaneously (government change, war, revolution). Micro risk targets a specific company or industry — and nationalization of assets is the textbook micro risk example: the government seizes a specific foreign company's operations. It doesn't destabilize every business; it targets one.

Nationalistic movements could contribute to instability broadly (macro), but the act of nationalizing a specific company's assets is targeted, making it micro. The size or drama of the action doesn't determine macro vs. micro — the scope (all industries vs. one company) does.

Rule: Macro = all industries affected. Micro = one company/sector targeted. Nationalization = micro, even though it sounds enormous.
Supply Chain — 2 misses
Q54 — Consumers prefer foreign-made over locally assembled = country-of-origin effect
MISSEDSupply Chain
❌ You chose
Quality-of-localization effect
✅ Correct
Country-of-origin effect

The country-of-origin effect describes consumer preference based on where a product was manufactured — regardless of actual quality. "Made in Germany" implies engineering precision. "Made in Switzerland" implies craftsmanship. When a company assembles locally but consumers still prefer the foreign-made version, they're responding to national origin reputation, not product testing.

"Quality-of-localization effect" is a fabricated term that doesn't exist in supply chain management. Always eliminate it immediately.

Rule: Consumer prefers foreign-made despite local assembly = country-of-origin effect. "Quality-of-localization effect" is fake — doesn't exist.
Q57 — Producer → distributor → retailer → consumer = indirect (not multi-level)
MISSEDSupply Chain
❌ You chose
Multi-level
✅ Correct
Indirect

Distribution channels are classified as either direct (0 intermediaries, producer → consumer) or indirect (1+ intermediaries between producer and consumer). Producer → distributor → retailer → consumer has two intermediary layers — that makes it an indirect channel.

"Multi-level" is not a standard distribution channel classification term. "Complex" isn't either. The only two standard answers are direct or indirect. Count intermediaries: 0 = direct, 1+ = indirect.

Rule: Direct = 0 intermediaries. Indirect = any intermediaries. "Multi-level" and "complex" are not real channel types — eliminate on sight.
Culture / HR — 1 miss
Q51 — Polycentric HR flaw: dual demands for shared resources (not cultural adjustment)
MISSEDCulture/HR
❌ You chose
Staff need adjustment to new culture
✅ Correct
Dual demands for shared resources

Polycentric HR = each subsidiary hires locally and operates with a high degree of independence. The documented, specific flaw is that independent subsidiaries each compete for the same corporate resources — budget, talent, technology. These dual demands for shared resources create internal inefficiency and tension between subsidiaries.

Cultural adjustment is the challenge for ethnocentric approaches (when home-country managers are sent abroad). Polycentric hires locally precisely to avoid cultural adjustment issues — that's its advantage, not its flaw.

Rule: Polycentric flaw = dual demands for shared resources (subsidiaries compete for same pool). Cultural adjustment = ethnocentric flaw (not polycentric).
Technology — 1 miss
Q70 — Cross-department systems not communicating → ERP (not MIS)
MISSEDTechnology
❌ You chose
Management information system
✅ Correct
Enterprise resource planning system (ERP)

The specific problem described is that information systems don't communicate across departments. This is precisely what ERP solves — it integrates all major business functions (finance, HR, supply chain, sales, procurement) into a single shared system so every department sees the same data in real time.

A management information system (MIS) is a general term for systems that provide management-level reporting. It doesn't specifically address the cross-departmental integration problem. ERP is the targeted, correct answer when the scenario is "departments can't share information with each other."

Rule: "Departments can't communicate across systems" = ERP. ERP is specifically about integration across the whole organization. MIS is too generic.
