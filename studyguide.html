Globalization — Foundations
What drives globalization — and what are its political costs?
Q1 Q2

Globalization = worldwide interconnections in products, technology, information, and jobs. The primary driver of higher globalization is reduced trade barriers — lower tariffs, relaxed regulations, open borders. Decreased FDI, higher business costs, and restricted migration all slow it.

The main negative political outcome is the creation of isolationist policies. When citizens feel threatened by foreign competition or cultural displacement, governments respond by turning inward — restricting immigration, raising tariffs, withdrawing from agreements.

Trap: "Increase in number of laws" sounds political but it's a generic governance effect, not a specific globalization consequence. The specific political backlash = isolationism.
CAGE Framework — Ghemawat's four dimensions of distance
Q3

CAGE analyzes how "distant" two countries are across four dimensions that affect cross-border trade and investment.

Culture
Administration
Geography
Economy

Administration covers political ties, colonial history, trade agreements, shared currency. It is NOT "Government" as its own factor. Economy covers income levels, resources, financial systems — NOT "Environment." Agriculture is not a CAGE factor at all.

Memory anchor: CAGE — Culture, Administration, Geography, Economy. Every wrong answer choice swaps in Agriculture, Government, or Environment. None of those belong.
Economic systems — traditional, market, command
Q5 Q6

A traditional economy relies on custom and historical practice — poor infrastructure, subsistence production, limited opportunity. Its globalization problem is a lower standard of living, not conflicts with other countries. It simply can't participate fully because the economic base isn't there.

A market economy = private ownership + price signals + firms seeking to maximize profits. Government abundance = command economy. Public ownership = command. Tradition-based production = traditional.

Outsourcing — home country drawback; Islamic law on business
Q4 Q7

When companies outsource manufacturing to lower-wage countries, the primary drawback for the home country = loss of manufacturing jobs. The company gains; domestic workers lose employment.

Islamic law (Sharia) directly impacts business by forbidding charging interest (riba). Islamic banking replaces interest with profit-sharing models (murabaha, musharaka). It does not prohibit sale-buyback, commercialize the legal system, or directly limit globalization.

International Institutions — IMF, World Bank, WTO
Three institutions — three completely different missions
Q8 Q9 Q10 Q11
IMF
Short-term financial stability. Helps countries with balance of payments / trade deficit problems. SDR reserve asset.
World Bank
Long-term development lending. Current focus: improving quality of life (health, education, poverty). Not structural development (old focus).
WTO
Standardizes trade rules, resolves disputes. Criticized for adopting labor standards protecting labor rights — seen as overstepping its trade mandate.

The IMF's Special Drawing Right (SDR) is valued based on the five most significant members' currencies: USD, Euro, Chinese Yuan, Japanese Yen, British Pound. Not gold. Not all members. Not just USD.

Q8 trap: Trade deficit financing = IMF, not World Bank. World Bank = development. Federal Reserve = U.S. only. International Finance Corporation = World Bank arm for private sector.
Trade Theory, Policy & Regional Integration
Comparative advantage — what happens when trade opens
Q12 Q13 Q14 Q17

Comparative advantage = specialize in what you produce most efficiently relative to others. When two countries open trade: jobs increase in comparative advantage industries. Jobs decrease in non-competitive sectors as they can't survive open competition.

NAFTA's economic impact = shift of jobs away from low comparative advantage industries. Countries reallocated labor to competitive sectors. Not net job loss, not GDP reduction.

A country using established technology infrastructure to produce a good will create barriers to entry for other nations — others can't easily replicate the capability.

When two countries open trade without tariffs → they're developing free trade policies with strategic partners. Not imposing restrictions on others. Not guaranteeing a surplus.

Tariffs, quotas, and protectionism — mechanisms and effects
Q15 Q16 Q18 Q19

A country imposing a tariff on imports is reasoning: protect its economy and give it opportunity for long-term expansion. Not to help the exporter. Not to divert trade.

When a country imposes quotas on imports, domestic prices increase. Quota restricts supply. Less supply + same demand = higher prices. Always.

Trade protectionism purpose = protect an infant industry. Shield a developing domestic sector until it can compete globally. Does NOT increase competition. Does not protect against import subsidies (that's countervailing duties).

To encourage foreign investment: host country should provide tax exemptions. Reduces investor cost and risk. Tariffs, protectionism, limiting privatization all deter investment.

Tariff types from glossary: Ad valorem = % of value. Specific = fixed $ per unit. Compound = fixed + % combined. Revenue tariffs = raise government revenue. Protective tariffs = shield domestic industry. Export tariffs = tax on outbound goods.
Regional economic integration — levels, customs unions, drawbacks
Q20 Q21 Q25

Integration levels from lowest to highest: Free Trade Area → Customs Union → Common Market → Economic Union → Political Union.

Customs Union key feature = free trade among members + unified trading policies (common external tariff) with non-members. This distinguishes it from a FTA (which has no common external tariff).

Drawback of regional trade agreements = they shift employment opportunities. Non-competitive industries lose jobs when exposed to more efficient regional competitors.

The broader economic drawback = specialization based on comparative advantage eliminates industries that can't compete — structural unemployment in those sectors.

NAFTA → USMCA; CAFTA; regional blocs
Q22 Q23

USMCA key provision: Almost half (~40-45%) of automobile parts must be produced by workers earning a minimum of $16/hour by 2023. This is a wage floor, not a regional origin requirement. Not 100% of parts. Not dairy. Not negotiation rights.

CAFTA (Central America Free Trade Agreement) = U.S. + Central American nations including Costa Rica. Relevant for any question involving U.S. investment in Central America.

CAFTA US + Central America

ASEAN Southeast Asia
Currency, Finance & Accounting
Currency strength and manufacturing profit
Q24

Firm in Country A manufactures in Country B, pays workers in Country B's currency. Maximum profit when Country A's currency is strong relative to Country B's. Each unit of your strong currency buys more of the weak currency = lower real labor costs = higher profit margin.

Rule: Strong home = cheap foreign labor. Weak home = expensive foreign labor. The strength relationship that matters is home (revenue) vs host (labor cost).
Currency instruments — forward contracts, futures, options, swaps
Q61

To reduce currency fluctuation effects over a defined period = use a forward contract. Locks in an exchange rate now for a future transaction, eliminating uncertainty.

Forward
Locked future rate. Firm commitment between two parties. Most common hedging tool.
Futures
Standardized contract, exchange-traded. Similar to forward but on an exchange.
Options
Right but NOT obligation to exchange at a set rate. Pays premium for flexibility.

Currency swap = two firms agree to exchange currencies now and reverse at a future date at a pre-agreed rate. Spot rate = current rate, immediate settlement. Hedging = using financial instruments to reduce adverse price/rate movements.

GAAP vs IFRS; financial statements; funding types; transfer pricing
Q58 Q59 Q60 Q62

GAAP = U.S. standard, governed by FASB. IFRS = international standard, governed by IASB. U.S. company + U.S. transaction = GAAP. Cross-border or foreign entity = IFRS. "Financial accounting reporting standards" and "General international accounting principles" do not exist — eliminate immediately.

Banks and investors use financial statements to decide whether to grant access to money. They assess creditworthiness and financial health. Not for marketing assessment, not for partnership recommendations.

Funding with low cost of capital and no maturity wait = loans. Bonds have maturity periods. Equity = ownership dilution. Angel = high-risk equity.

Transfer pricing = shifting assets/income to subsidiaries in lower-tax countries. Effect on the subsidiary: lowered profit on paper. The subsidiary records less income than it would otherwise, reducing the parent's overall tax burden.

Political risk — macro vs micro
Q63

Macro risk = political factors affecting overall country stability, impacting ANY industry regardless of type. Examples: change in government, civil war, revolution.

Micro risk = political risk targeting a specific industry or company. Government nationalization of assets = micro risk. It seizes specific companies or sectors, not the entire economy.

Trap: Nationalization sounds big and macro but it's micro — it targets specific assets/industries. Government leadership change = macro (affects everything).
Law — Antitrust, Contracts, Intellectual Property
Antitrust laws, monopoly creation, and CISG
Q26 Q27 Q28

Price-fixing, price discrimination, trade restraints, and monopolization all violate antitrust (competition/antimonopoly) laws. The Sherman Antitrust Act (1890) was the first U.S. antitrust law targeting price-controlling cartels.

A monopoly is created by control of natural resources — owning the only source of a critical input = market control. High interest rates, educated employees, and vertical integration alone don't create monopolies.

The CISG (UN Convention on Contracts for the International Sale of Goods) provides gap-fillers for unstated terms in cross-border commercial contracts. UCC = U.S. domestic contracts. Common Law = general tradition. CISG applies specifically when companies from different countries are transacting.

IP protections from glossary: Patents = inventions (time-limited). Trademarks = brand identifiers (word, name, symbol). Copyrights = original creative works. Trade secrets = kept private indefinitely. TRIPS = WTO agreement governing international IP protection.
Ethics, CSR & Corporate Governance
CSR — goals, strategy, ethical standards, grease payments
Q29 Q30 Q31 Q32 Q33

Green packaging costs more → the concern is decreased profitability. Not reputation damage (they're responding to demands). Not environmental damage (they're reducing it).

CSR goal when going global = increase shareholder trust via high ethical standards. Long-term trust-building is the core CSR rationale.

Global CSR strategy = engage stakeholders on air quality. Broad, multi-stakeholder, environmental = global CSR. Funding local charities and career days = local activities, not global CSR.

Grease payment dilemma = most challenging aspect is that ethical behaviors are not standardized across the world. Acceptable in one culture, prohibited in another — this is the textbook cross-cultural ethics problem.

CEO enforcing mission/values regardless of situation = code of ethics. CSR = broader societal obligations. Transparency = openness. Code of ethics = behavioral standards all employees follow consistently.

Organizational Structures
Functional, divisional, matrix, teams — full comparison
Q34 Q35 Q36 Q37 Q38
Functional
Departments by business function: marketing, HR, IT, production, finance. Classic structure.
Divisional
Groups by product, process, or geographic region. Each division is semi-autonomous.
Matrix
Dual reporting lines (geographic + product). Used in high-tech/engineering for limited-duration complex projects.
Teams
Mutual accountability, creativity, productivity through shared ownership. Drives innovation.
Product dept.
By goods/services offered (washers, dryers, refrigerators). Not by customer type or region.
Centralized
Decision authority at top. Result: increased consistency in operations across all subsidiaries.
Q36 trap: "Mutual accountability" = Teams, not Matrix. Matrix is for complex dual-reporting projects. Teams is for shared ownership and creative accountability.
Market Entry Strategies
Entry strategy spectrum — low to high commitment
Q39–Q45
1 Exporting
2 Licensing
3 Franchising
4 Strategic Alliance
5 Joint Venture
6 Acquisition/Subsidiary
7 Greenfield

Subsidiary = buy an existing company in the target country and run it. Direct operating presence. High commitment, high control.

Greenfield venture = build from scratch where nothing existed. Provides ALL assets, assumes ALL responsibility, MAXIMUM risk, SLOWEST entry. No prior business to leverage.

Exporting via distributor = still exporting. The company ships its products; a local distributor handles distribution. Not franchising, not joint venture.

Exporting disadvantage = high transportation costs. Quality control is maintained (you still manufacture it). IP sharing = licensing, not exporting.

Decentralized manufacturing = solution to weather/shipping delays. Build locally, eliminate dependency on cross-ocean shipping.

Standardization = same branding/product everywhere. Adaptation = customized for local markets. Glocalization = adapts locally while keeping global identity.

Licensing vs Franchising: Licensing = right to use a product/IP for a fee. Franchising = right to use brand, name, and entire business process in exchange for % of profits. Franchising is more comprehensive.
Culture — Hofstede's Dimensions & HR Strategy
All 6 Hofstede dimensions — precise definitions
Q46 Q47 Q48
Power Distance
How agreeably society accepts hierarchical differences. High = authority unquestioned. Low = egalitarian.
Uncertainty Avoidance
Amount of ambiguity a society tolerates. High = rigid rules. Low = comfortable with risk/change.
Individualism
Self and close family vs broader group. High = everyone fends for themselves. Low = collectivist.
Masculinity
Achievement/competition vs cooperation/quality of life. High = assertiveness valued. Low = feminine traits valued.
Long-term Orientation
Perseverance and future planning vs immediate gratification and tradition. High = focused on the future.
Indulgence vs Restraint
Degree of freedom and spontaneity valued. High indulgence = enjoys life. High restraint = controls gratification.
Q46 trap: Hierarchy acceptance = Power Distance, NOT Long-term orientation. Each dimension has exactly ONE concept. Don't mix them.
Cultural dimensions in practice — evaluation, repatriation, HR staffing
Q49 Q50 Q51

A manager who fully adapts to Indonesian workplace harmony = receives a very favorable performance evaluation. In collectivist, high-harmony cultures, cultural integration signals trustworthiness and respect.

Repatriation disorientation = caused by changes in work culture and customs. The employee readjusted to the foreign culture and must now readjust back to their home culture — reverse culture shock.

Ethnocentric = home-country nationals in all key positions. Polycentric = local subsidiaries hire locally. Disadvantage of polycentric: dual demands for shared resources — independent subsidiaries compete for the same corporate budget and capabilities. Geocentric = best person globally regardless of nationality.

Q51 trap: "Less flexibility adjusting to local conditions" = ethnocentric flaw. Polycentric's specific flaw = dual resource demands from competing independent units.
Global Production, Supply Chain & Distribution
Lean manufacturing, value-to-weight, country-of-origin, distribution
Q52–Q57

Lean manufacturing = eliminating all non-value-adding activities and waste. Reducing manufacturing waste = lean. Just-in-time (JIT) = receiving materials only when needed for production — a subset of lean focused on inventory minimization.

Value-to-weight ratio = value per pound. Low ratio items (heavy/cheap) must be produced near markets — shipping costs exceed value. High ratio items (semiconductors, pharma) can ship anywhere. Key factor for facility location to minimize shipping costs.

Country-of-origin effect = consumer perception of where a product is made influences purchase decision. Assembling locally but consumers preferring foreign-made = this phenomenon.

Indirect channel advantage = reduces up-front costs using an existing channel. Intermediary has established relationships and logistics. Direct channel advantages = control and ownership.

Direct consumer connection = 0 intermediaries. Producer → Distributor → Retailer → Consumer = indirect channel. Not "complex," not "multi-level" — those aren't standard terms.

Pull vs Push model: Pull = decisions based on customer needs/demand. Push = decisions based on product/forecast. Pull = demand-driven. Push = supply-driven.
Technology in Global Business
Digital divide, AI, blockchain, 4th Industrial Revolution, ERP
Q64–Q70

Digital divide stages: Economic (can't afford) → Accessibility (can't access) → Usability (can't use skillfully) → Empowerment (has access and skills but doesn't value it). Community with skills and access that doesn't value technology = empowerment stage.

Cloud-based file share = 24/7 global access for distributed teams. Email attachments aren't persistent. Social media = public. Desktop publishing = local.

AI deep learning = machines learning from unstructured data without explicit programming. Example: machines recognizing product defects over time. Biometrics ≠ deep learning. Bar code sorting ≠ deep learning.

Blockchain = secure, decentralized, private storage of data accessible globally. Not AI (different function). Not antivirus (protection only).

Fourth Industrial Revolution = autonomous cars, 3D printing, biometrics, nanotechnology, quantum computing. The convergence of digital, physical, and biological technologies. (1st=Steam, 2nd=Electricity, 3rd=Computers/internet, 4th=Everything converging).

Education = slowest sector to adopt AI. Institutional inertia, public-sector dominance, and regulatory caution all slow adoption relative to financial services, healthcare, and transportation.

Enterprise Resource Planning (ERP) = integrated system allowing all departments to communicate and share data. Solution when information systems don't communicate across departments. (Q70 — the one you got right.)

⚠️ High-Risk Confusion Patterns — D080 / QHC1 These are the conceptual traps most likely to cost you points on the OA. Each card shows the wrong instinct, the correct answer, and a single rule to lock it in.
1Institution confusion
IMF vs World Bank — trade deficit financing
Wrong instinct
World Bank (it loans money, right?)
Correct
IMF — balance of payments / trade deficits
Rule: IMF = short-term financial stability, trade deficits, exchange rates. World Bank = long-term quality of life development. WTO = trade rules. Three completely separate missions.

Sounds right
Supporting structural development (old focus)
Current focus
Improving quality of life in developing nations
Rule: The World Bank evolved from infrastructure/structural focus to quality of life outcomes (health, education, poverty reduction). Current focus = quality of life.

Common wrong pick
Long-term orientation (for hierarchy acceptance)
Correct

5Adjacent wrong answer

Intuitive pick
Matrix (people working together on projects)
Correct
Teams structure = mutual accountability + creativity
Rule: Teams = shared ownership, mutual accountability, creativity. Matrix = dual reporting lines for limited-duration complex projects. "Mutual accountability" → Teams every time.
6Adjacent wrong answer
Greenfield vs Subsidiary — both direct presence, different mechanism
Common confusion
Greenfield when scenario says "buys and runs"
Correct mapping
Subsidiary = acquire existing. Greenfield = build from scratch.
Rule: Greenfield = provides ALL assets, no prior business exists. Subsidiary = buys an existing company and operates it. "Highest risk + slowest entry + all assets" = Greenfield. "Buys and runs existing" = Subsidiary.
7Adjacent wrong answer
Exporting with a distributor is still exporting — not franchising
Tempting wrong pick
Franchising or joint venture
Correct
Exporting (indirect channel via distributor)
Rule: If the company still manufactures at home and ships overseas, it's exporting — regardless of who handles local distribution. Franchising = granting rights to your brand/process. The mechanism (who distributes) doesn't change the strategy type.
8Cause vs Effect
Quota → domestic prices INCREASE, not decrease
Wrong instinct
Prices stay the same or decrease
Correct
Prices INCREASE — restricted supply + same demand
Rule: Import quota = less foreign supply available. Less supply + same demand = higher domestic prices. Quotas protect producers by raising prices. They always raise consumer prices.
9Scope error
Polycentric flaw vs Ethnocentric flaw — different weaknesses
Ethnocentric's flaw (not polycentric)
Less flexibility adjusting to local conditions
Polycentric's actual flaw
Dual demands for shared resources
Rule: Polycentric = local subsidiaries act independently → they compete for the same corporate resources (budget, talent, tech). Ethnocentric = top-down → lacks local flexibility. Each has its own distinct weakness.
10Definition confusion
Micro risk — nationalization targets specific assets, not the whole country
Feels like macro
Government nationalization of assets (big action)
It's actually micro
Micro risk = targets specific industry/company
Rule: Macro = affects all industries (political instability, war, leadership change). Micro = targets a specific company or industry (nationalizing YOUR oil field, seizing YOUR assets). Nationalization = micro even though it sounds big.
11Institution confusion
GAAP vs IFRS — jurisdiction determines which applies, fake options exist
Fake options to eliminate
"Financial accounting reporting standards" / "General international accounting principles"
Real options only
GAAP (U.S.) or IFRS (international)
Rule: Only two real options exist. U.S. company + U.S. transaction = GAAP. International entity or cross-border = IFRS. Eliminate any option that isn't one of those two real standards.
12Adjacent wrong answer
USMCA provision — wage floor (~50% at $16/hr), NOT 100% regional origin
Sounds right
100% of auto parts must be made within NAFTA region
Correct
~half of parts by workers earning $16+/hr by 2023
Rule: USMCA's specific auto provision = wage floor, not origin percentage. The threshold is ~40-45% of parts, produced by workers at minimum $16/hour. Memorize: "almost half" + "$16/hour" + "2023."
13Definition confusion
Digital divide empowerment — has access and skills but doesn't value it
Feels like this
Accessibility stage (they have the tech...)
Correct stage
Empowerment — has everything, lacks motivation/value
Rule: If they HAVE access AND skills but DON'T use it → Empowerment gap. The solution is showing value, not providing equipment or training. Economic = can't afford. Accessibility = can't reach. Usability = can't use. Empowerment = won't use.
