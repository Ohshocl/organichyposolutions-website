Section 1 — Elements of Distribution, Logistics & Transportation
Logistics vs Distribution vs Supply Chain — scope hierarchy
S1 Core

Logistics = planning and managing the flow of goods, services, and information from origin to consumption. It's the broader discipline that encompasses transportation, warehousing, and inventory.

Distribution = the subset of logistics focused specifically on getting finished goods from manufacturer/warehouse out to customers. Multiple distribution channels and alternate ways of delivering products exist.

Supply Chain = the widest scope — includes all parties and processes from raw material suppliers through manufacturers to end customers. Logistics is one function within supply chain management.

Effective managers integrate logistics into the distribution system in relation to logistics management. Logistics strategy is derived from the corporate business plan — it doesn't exist independently.
Logistics design strategies — 4 characteristics of distribution system development
S1 CoreQ1 Q12

There are four main characteristics of a product that affect distribution system development: product value, product density (volume-to-weight), product handling requirements, and demand risk.

Operational decisions that affect a shipping company's functionality are decisions about day-to-day running — vehicle maintenance is an operational decision (not strategic). Location/size of depots = strategic/tactical. Fleet capacity management = tactical.

Operational
Day-to-day: Vehicle maintenance, picking assignments, scheduling
Strategic
Long-term: Location/size of depots, network design, fleet composition
Q1 Trap: "Which operational decision affects a shipping company's functionality?" Answer = Vehicle maintenance. "Location and size of depots" is strategic/tactical, not operational.
Logistics providers — 1PL, 2PL, 3PL, 4PL
S1 CoreQ2 Q3
3PL
Third-party logistics: provides transportation, warehousing, specific parts of supply chain. Outsourcing specific functions.
4PL
Fourth-party logistics: manages the ENTIRE supply chain. Orchestrates 3PLs. Solutions delivered across the entire supply chain — not just specific parts.

Why outsource? Primary reason = to reduce logistics costs. Not to increase assets, not to make logistics a core competency (outsourcing does the opposite — it removes it from core).

3PL = specific parts. 4PL = entire supply chain. 4PL is NOT the same as LLP (Lead Logistics Provider) — though similar, they are distinct terms.
Transportation modes — choosing the right one
S1 CoreQ4 Q5 Q6

Air = optimal for high-value, time-sensitive, fragile items over long distances. An expensive antique vase shipped a very long distance = Air. Why: speed + security + handling standards for fragile items.

Road transport selection factors: The two key factors for selecting the appropriate road transport type for a large shipment = weight and volume of cargo. Not length/breadth, not size/shape.

Air
High-value, fragile, time-sensitive, long distance. Fastest. Most expensive per kg.
Sea
Bulk, heavy, non-urgent. Cheapest per unit. Slowest.
Rail
Heavy bulk inland. Cost-effective for large volumes overland.
Road
Flexible, door-to-door, short-medium distance. Most common for last-mile.

Marine fuel surcharge: Variations in marine (sea) fuel pricing are accounted for by the Bunker Adjustment Surcharge (BAS). Not carrier security, not peak season, not currency adjustment.

Volume-to-weight ratio — distribution efficiency
S1 CoreQ7

Distribution efficiency is measured by the volume-to-weight ratio. A lower ratio means the product is dense and heavy relative to its size — it fills weight capacity before space capacity, making each trip more efficient.

Given ratios: A=100, B=600, C=1000, D=250 — Product A (ratio=100) is most efficient because you can load the most weight per unit of space. Low ratio = dense = efficient.

Rule: Lowest volume-to-weight ratio = most efficient for distribution. The product fits the most weight into the least space per trip.

S1 CoreQ8 Q13 Q14
Introductory

Growth
Greatest positive slope — fastest rate of sales increase. Market adoption accelerating rapidly.

Own-brand substitution (supermarket producing its own identical products when branded stock runs low) = decreases logistics costs. It reduces supplier dependence and simplifies the supply chain.

Operations strategies — the 4 Vs
S1 CoreQ9 Q11

When a company faces large fluctuations in demand, it should use a Variability-based production strategy — designed specifically to handle demand uncertainty and variation.

Volume-based
High-volume, standardized production. Economies of scale. Low variety.
Variety-based
Wide product range. High customization. Lower volumes per SKU.

Which performance type minimizes waste? = Quality performance. Quality-focused operations eliminate defects and rework, which are forms of waste. Not time (speed), not flexibility, not dependability.

Manufacturing processes — matching process to product
S1 CoreQ10

A large chemical company producing water treatment chemicals for municipal facilities should use Continuous production — because the product is identical, high-volume, non-stop, and requires a consistent uninterrupted process.

Project
One-off unique items. Long duration. Ship, building, custom machine.
Batch
Groups of similar items. Bakery, pharmaceuticals in batches.
Mass
High volume, standardized. Automotive assembly.
Continuous
Non-stop 24/7. Chemicals, oil refining, paper mills. Identical product, never stops.
Logistics network reengineering — cost vs service tradeoff
S1 CoreQ12

When a company reengineers its logistics network by lowering costs, the consequence is that the number of facilities decreases. Fewer warehouses/distribution centers = lower fixed costs, but typically results in longer delivery lead times and lower responsiveness.

Cost-service tradeoff: Lowering costs → fewer facilities → lower service level (longer lead times, less responsiveness). Increasing service → more facilities → higher costs.
Logistics types — Inbound, Outbound, Reverse
S1 CoreQ15
Inbound
Materials/goods coming INTO the organization from suppliers. Procurement logistics.
Outbound
Finished goods going OUT to customers. Distribution logistics.
Reverse
Returns, recycling, waste management. Goods flowing backwards through supply chain. Company improving recycling = Reverse logistics.
Distribution operations — Break-bulk, Cross-docking, Postponement
S1 CoreQ16 Q37
Break-bulk
Receives large shipments, breaks them into smaller ones for distribution. Large in → small out.
Cross-docking
Goods transferred directly from inbound to outbound with no storage. Minimizes warehouse time.
Postponement
Delaying final configuration/customization until closer to demand point.
Echelon
Multi-level distribution: production → warehouse → local DCs → customers. Sequential layers.

Distribution process moving goods from production → warehouse → local DCs → customers = Echelon distribution (multi-level sequential flow).

Government transport actions — Incentive vs Regulation vs Control
S1 CoreQ17

A government setting a road tax exemption for electric vehicles = Incentive. It encourages desired behavior through a benefit/reward, not by mandating compliance.

Incentive
Rewards desired behavior (tax exemptions, subsidies, grants). Voluntary. EV road tax exemption.
Regulation
Mandatory rules with penalties for non-compliance. Emissions standards, speed limits.
Control
Direct government management/ownership of transport infrastructure.
Target
Policy goals/objectives without specific enforcement mechanism.
Empty miles — minimizing backhaul waste
S1 CoreQ18 Q36

The most efficient way to minimize empty miles = Pick up lots from suppliers AFTER drop-off to customers. This eliminates the return trip being empty by using it productively.

Separately: A vehicle returning empty after every delivery can be optimized by collecting raw materials on the return trip — same principle, backhaul utilization.

Backhaul optimization: Never run empty. Use the return journey to collect from suppliers. Combines delivery and pickup into one trip = half the empty miles.
Section 2 — Laws, Regulations & Cross-Border Operations
Cross-border logistics decisions affecting customs tax liability
S2 CoreQ19

The cross-border decision that affects customs tax liabilities = Production location. Where goods are produced determines country of origin, which determines tariff rates and preferential trade agreements.

Country of origin is tied to production location → affects which tariff schedule applies → directly impacts customs tax liability. Production lot size = inventory decision. Production quality/reliability = operations decisions unrelated to customs.

S2 CoreQ20 Q31

Prepares and submits customs declarations on behalf of importers/exporters. Acts as intermediary with customs authorities.
BEST
Border Enforcement Security Task Force — improves SAFETY in international trade and supply chain.

Manages port/terminal facilities. Loading/unloading of vessels.

International trade laws — TFA, Kyoto Convention, FCPA
S2 CoreQ21 Q22 Q26

Trade Facilitation Agreement (TFA) = the international law that focuses on harmonizing customs procedures throughout the world. WTO agreement streamlining global customs processes.

Kyoto Customs Convention goal = to make customs procedures more predictable. It creates transparency, simplification, and standardization of customs procedures — NOT to control businesses or build opacity.

Corrupt Practice Act / FCPA (1977) = involves the use of corporations making unsanctioned payments both inside AND outside the country. It prohibits bribing foreign government officials. Key point: applies to corporations AND individuals, and covers both domestic and international unsanctioned payments.

FCPA Trap: One wrong answer says "multinational corporations but not individuals" — WRONG. The FCPA applies to individuals too.
Customs tariff rates — commodity codes
S2 CoreQ23

The factor that helps define and categorize customs tariff rates = Commodity code (also called Harmonized System code or HS code). Every product gets a numerical code that determines its tariff rate.

Commodity code (HS code) = the universal numerical classification system for goods. The code determines tariff rates, not the transportation mode or customer code. Goods valuation also matters but commodity classification comes first.
Border clearance best practices
S2 CoreQ24

To assist international agencies at the border and ensure efficient clearance of products = Improving declarations and documentation. Accurate, complete paperwork is the primary enabler of smooth border crossings.

Customs authorities need correct documentation to process shipments. Poor or incomplete declarations are the #1 cause of delays. "Improving declarations and documentation" directly addresses this.
Customs & Trade Compliance Manager — key skills
S2 CoreQ25

A customs and trade compliance manager needs = The ability to ensure successful systems deployment for vigorous trade operations. This role manages technology systems, processes, and regulatory compliance that enable efficient cross-border trade.

Supply cost specific to international trade
S2 CoreQ27

The key supply cost component specific to international trade = Cross-border tariffs cost. Tariffs are unique to international trade — domestic trade has no equivalent. Rework cost, quality cost, and direct taxation exist in domestic operations too.

Agile supply chain strategies — Quick Response, ECR
S2 CoreQ28 Q29

Supply chain strategy used to address demand changes using an agile method = Quick Response. Quick Response shortens replenishment lead times and increases responsiveness to actual demand.

Efficient Consumer Response (ECR) benefit in global trade = Improved service level. ECR focuses on improving the efficiency of the entire supply chain from manufacturer to consumer, primarily measured by service level improvement.

Quick Response
Agile strategy. Short replenishment cycles. Fast reaction to demand signals. Apparel industry origin.
ECR
Efficient Consumer Response. Grocery industry origin. Focuses on supply chain efficiency + consumer service level.

Inventory types — Cycle, Safety, Pipeline, In-process, Speculative
S3 CoreQ33 Q34 Q38 Q39
Cycle Stock
Stock equivalent to typical regular demand — meets frequent customer purchases for a set period. Q34: one quarter of regular demand = Cycle stock.
Safety Stock
Buffer stock held to protect against demand uncertainty or supply delays. Extra above cycle stock.
In-process
Partially finished stock held close to production for rapid completion and dispatch. Q33: WIP held at nearby supplier for 1-hour delivery = In-process.
Pipeline
Stock in transit between locations. "On the way" inventory.
Speculative
Stock bought in advance anticipating price rises or shortages.
Seasonal
Built up ahead of predictable seasonal demand peaks.

Inventory cost driver: A firm rapidly increasing its variety of goods (needing more SKUs) → rising inventory costs = Increased product proliferation. More product lines = more SKUs = more inventory to hold.

Shortage cost: Penalty for not holding adequate stock = Shortage cost (element of inventory-holding cost).

Forecasting methods — Moving Average, Exponential Smoothing, Causal
S3 CoreQ32

Calculating average demand for the next period based on actual demand from the previous two years = Moving Average. It averages historical data over a defined time window.

Moving Average
Average of actual demand over a fixed historical period. Simple, uses past data directly.
Exponential Smoothing
Weighted average giving more weight to recent data. Automatically adjusts for trends.
Causal
Uses external variables (weather, GDP, advertising spend) to predict demand.
Experimental
Based on market testing / consumer research. Qualitative.
Pareto analysis — A/B/C line classification
S3 CoreQ35

Products sold quickly at 90% availability across all DCs = A line in Pareto analysis.

A Line
Top 20% of products generating ~80% of revenue. Fastest moving. Highest availability target (90%+). Priority at all DCs.
B Line
Mid-tier. Regular movers. Moderate availability (75-85%).
C Line
Slow movers. 50% of SKUs, ~5% of revenue. Lower availability targets, fewer locations.
D Line
Obsolete or nearly discontinued. Minimal stock.
Operations control systems — principles and benchmarking
S3 CoreQ40 Q41 Q42 Q43

Operating control system = evaluates KPIs related to cost centers and operations standards, compares actual vs planned. Tracks performance variance and drives corrective action.

Validity = the principle that data covers ALL stages of production and distribution necessary for decisions. When data is missing stages = Validity is compromised.

Measurement system best practice when managers at different decision levels contribute = Hierarchy of needs. Design the system around the decision-making levels.

Benchmarking = comparing your performance against world-class standards from other industries to identify gaps and drive continuous improvement.

Malcolm Baldridge National Quality Award criteria = the US framework based on productivity and operational performance excellence officially recognized in the United States.

Key benchmarking terms: Benchmarking = comparing vs best-in-class. Quality Function Deployment = benchmarking system focused on customer requirements and supplier performance.
Outsourcing logistics — 3PL decisions and impacts
S3 CoreQ52 Q53 Q54 Q55 Q56 Q57 Q58 Q59

Impact of outsourcing distribution/logistics: Focusing on core business = decreases logistics decision-making control (you give up control when you outsource). Investment freed up = investment opportunities in more profitable business areas.

Multiuser 3PL advantage: Serves multiple clients = economies of scale can be achieved with other firms (shared assets reduce per-unit cost).

Risk of outsourcing to unknown start-up: Strong-brand firm + unknown 3PL = the third-party brand could interfere with the firm's brand. Customer experience is affected by the 3PL's reputation.

First step when outsourcing operations: = Identify service requirements and costs. Before selecting a provider, you must define what you need and what it should cost.

Dedicated 3PL charges more because of: = Specialization of knowledge and expertise. When a provider serves one client exclusively, they develop deep specialized expertise worth a premium.

Gainsharing: Provider gets % of profits when exceeding targets = Gainsharing-based activity incentive. Aligns provider incentives with client performance goals.

Performance tracking in outsourcing: Monitoring targets via open-book audits with decision-makers = Performance tracking.

Warehouse management systems (WMS) — which system for which task
S4 CoreQ44 Q60 Q70
WMS
Controls all warehouse activities: automated equipment, RF communications, picking, put-away, inventory. Q44 and Q70.
TMS
Transportation Management System. Plans and optimizes shipping routes, carrier selection, freight audit.
YMS
Yard Management System. Controls vehicle movements in the yard, dock assignments.
OMS
Order Management System. Customer-facing order processing, multi-channel orders.

Automated storage/retrieval system (AS/RS) = connects picking operations to WMS to eliminate human intervention. Fully automated storage and retrieval.

Racking and storage systems — matching to requirement
S4 CoreQ61
Cantilevered Racking
For long, awkward items (timber, pipes, turbine blades). Arms extend from upright with no front column — accommodates length. Minimal investment cost.
Powered Mobile Racking
High-density storage. Aisles on wheels. Highest cost, best space utilization.
Drive-in/Drive-through
Forklift drives into racking. LIFO (drive-in) or FIFO (drive-through). Bulk storage.

Picking — most labor-intensive warehouse activity
S4 CoreQ62 Q63 Q64

Picking = the most labor-intensive and cost-intensive warehouse activity. A company wanting to reduce direct labor costs should focus on picking first.

Why do two pickers have different productivity (100 vs 200 lines/hour)? = Picking method. Different methods (single order, batch, zone, wave picking) produce dramatically different productivity rates for individual pickers.

Why do two WAREHOUSES have different average picking productivity (X vs 2X orders/picker/hour)? = Picking equipment usage. At warehouse level, equipment (conveyors, pick-to-light, voice-directed, automated systems) explains the gap, not individual method.

Trap: Pickers (individual level) → Picking method. Warehouses (facility level) → Picking equipment usage. Don't confuse the level of analysis.
Warehouse planning horizons and metrics
S4 CoreQ65 Q66 Q67

Short-term planning = Workload planning based on order receipts. Responding to actual orders received = short-term (daily/weekly). Budget planning = long-term. Capacity planning = medium-term. Demand planning = medium-term.

Metric to improve warehouse employee safety = Days without an accident. Tracks safety performance directly. Stock integrity = accuracy metric. Customer service levels = service metric.

Put-away cycle time and stock turnover ratio calculations
S4 CoreQ68 Q69

Put-away cycle time = time from stock RECEIPT to STORAGE LOCATION (not from order receipt). Stock received 12th, moved to storage 15th = 3 days put-away cycle time. (15 - 12 = 3).

Q68 Trap: Don't include the order processing time (16th-17th). Put-away = receipt to storage only. Stock received day 12, stored day 15 = 3 days.

Stock turnover ratio = how many times inventory is sold/used in a year. Higher ratio = lower inventory held (turns over faster). W=4, X=5, Y=8, Z=10 → Warehouse Z holds the lowest inventory (turns over 10x/year = very lean).

Higher stock turnover = lower inventory = leaner operation. Z turns over 10x = holds the least stock on hand at any time.

Dependent vs Independent Demand — Push vs Pull systems
S3 Core
Independent Demand
Demand for finished goods — driven by external customers. Cannot be derived from other product demand. Forecasting required.
Dependent Demand
Demand for components/parts derived from independent demand. If you need 100 bikes, you need 200 wheels. Calculated, not forecasted.
Push System
Production/distribution based on forecast. Goods pushed downstream before demand occurs. Risk: overstock if forecast wrong.
Pull System
Production/distribution triggered by actual demand. Goods pulled through supply chain by customer orders. Risk: stockout if demand spikes.
Just-in-time (JIT) is a pull system — production triggered by actual demand, minimizing inventory. MRP (Material Requirements Planning) uses dependent demand logic to calculate component needs from a production schedule.

📚 Rushton Handbook — Chapters 1,2,4,5 (Section 1 Source Material)
Ch.1 — Scope & definition of logistics and distribution
Rushton Ch.1Section 1

Logistics is the process of strategically managing the procurement, movement, and storage of materials, parts, and finished inventory — and the related information flows — from point of origin through to point of consumption. It encompasses both physical flow and information flow.

Distribution is the management of the movement and storage of finished goods from the end of the production line to the consumer. Distribution is the outbound-facing subset of logistics.

Logistics costs as a percentage of GDP vary significantly by country — typically 8–15% of GDP in developed economies. As a percentage of sales turnover, logistics costs average around 10–15% for most companies but vary widely by industry.

Key Rushton distinction: Logistics = total flow management (inbound, internal, outbound). Distribution = outbound only (finished goods to customer). Supply chain = everything including suppliers, manufacturers, and customers.

The 7 Rights of Customer Service (from the book): the right product, in the right quantity, in the right condition, at the right place, at the right time, to the right customer, at the right cost.

Ch.2 — Total logistics concept, trade-offs, and planning hierarchy
Rushton Ch.2Section 1

The total logistics concept recognizes that logistics functions must be managed as an integrated whole rather than as separate silos. Optimizing one function in isolation often sub-optimizes the total system — this is the core trade-off principle.

Logistics planning hierarchy:

  • Strategic (3–5 years): Network design, number and location of warehouses, make-or-buy decisions, modal choice
  • Tactical (1–2 years): Fleet size, warehouse layout, staffing levels, system selection
  • Operational (daily/weekly): Vehicle scheduling, order picking, inventory replenishment, daily staff deployment

Financial impact of logistics: Logistics affects both sides of the balance sheet. It reduces assets (less inventory, fewer facilities) and reduces costs (lower storage, transport). This improves Return on Investment (ROI) in multiple ways.

Trade-off principle: Reducing warehouses lowers fixed costs but increases transport costs and lead times. The total cost optimum is the point where combined costs are minimized — not where any single cost is minimized.
Ch.4 — Channels of distribution and outsourcing decision
Rushton Ch.4Section 1

Distribution channels are the routes goods take from producer to end consumer. Options include: direct to consumer, via retailers, via wholesalers, or via agents/brokers. "Long" channels have multiple intermediaries. "Short" channels go direct.

Channel selection factors: product characteristics (value, perishability, hazard), market coverage needed, customer service requirements, cost, and control.

Outsourcing channels (3PL/4PL): Global 3PL market is substantial — outsourcing split by region shows Europe at approximately 60% outsourced logistics spend. The decision to outsource is driven by: cost reduction, access to expertise, flexibility, and focus on core competency.

Dedicated 3PL
Resources exclusively allocated to one client. Higher cost per unit but specialized knowledge. Can charge premium.
Multi-user 3PL
Shared resources across multiple clients. Lower cost through economies of scale. Less customization.
Ch.5 — Key issues and challenges: 4PLs, freight exchanges, environment
Rushton Ch.5Section 1

Fourth-party logistics (4PL) is distinguished from 3PL in that the 4PL manages the entire supply chain on behalf of the client, often orchestrating multiple 3PLs. The 4PL's value is integration and information management across the whole chain.

Freight exchanges are digital online platforms for buying and selling freight capacity — similar to a stock exchange but for logistics services. Different exchanges are differentiated by: mode, geography, buyer type, and whether spot or contract rates.

Key external challenges identified by Rushton:

  • Environmental pressures — carbon reduction, emissions regulations, green logistics
  • Security requirements — supply chain security post-9/11, C-TPAT, AEO programs
  • Omnichannel retailing — simultaneous management of multiple fulfilment channels
  • Globalization — longer supply chains, more cross-border complexity
  • Urbanization — last-mile delivery in congested city centres
📚 Rushton Handbook — Chapters 6,8,9,10,11,13 (Planning & Procurement)
Ch.6 — Logistics design strategy, product characteristics, and the PLC
Rushton Ch.6Planning

A logistics design strategy starts from the corporate strategic plan and works down through logistics network design. Key external pressures include: cost reduction demands, customer service expectations, globalization, and environmental regulations.

Four product characteristics that affect logistics design (DIRECTLY TESTED on your exam):

1. Volume/weight ratio
Low ratio = dense/heavy = fills weight capacity. High ratio = bulky/light = fills cubic capacity. Affects vehicle and storage efficiency.
2. Value/weight ratio
High value per kg = worth spending more on premium transport/security. Low value = must minimize cost.
3. Substitutability
Can customers easily switch to a competitor's product? High substitutability = high availability needed. Low = customer will wait.
4. Risk (hazard/perishability)
Perishable goods need cold chain and speed. Hazardous goods need specialist handling and compliance.

Product Life Cycle (PLC) logistics implications: Introduction = limited distribution, high service cost per unit. Growth = rapid network expansion needed. Maturity = cost efficiency focus, consolidation. Decline = selective distribution, inventory wind-down.

Ch.8 — Supply chain segmentation (product and demand)
Rushton Ch.8Planning

Supply chain segmentation recognizes that different products and customers require different logistics approaches. One-size-fits-all logistics is inefficient. The three main segmentation dimensions are:

Product segmentation
By throughput velocity and value density. High throughput + high value = most critical to manage carefully. Pareto: top 20% of SKUs often 80% of volume.
Demand/supply segmentation
Predictable vs variable demand. Stable demand = lean/efficient supply chain. Variable demand = agile/responsive supply chain.
Marketing segmentation
Different customer types need different service levels. Premium customers may warrant dedicated service. Standard customers = cost-efficient.

The Pareto principle (80/20 rule) is the foundation of ABC analysis in inventory and distribution: roughly 20% of products generate 80% of sales value. A-class items get priority in picking, storage proximity, and availability targets.

Ch.9 — Logistics network planning: depot numbers, costs, and trade-offs
Rushton Ch.9Planning

The key insight from logistics network planning is that different costs move in opposite directions as you add or remove depots:

Costs that INCREASE with more depots
Warehouse/storage costs. Inventory holding costs. Information systems costs. Fixed overhead per site.
Costs that DECREASE with more depots
Local delivery (secondary transport) costs. Customer lead times. Last-mile distance. Responsiveness improves.

Primary transport = trunking from factory/national DC to regional DCs. Cost DECREASES as you add more depots (shorter trunking runs)... actually it's complex — primary goes UP, secondary goes DOWN. The total cost optimum is typically at a moderate number of depots.

Network reengineering rule: Reducing depots to lower costs = fewer facilities, lower fixed costs, but longer delivery distances and lower customer service. This is the pre-assessment's correct answer for "what happens when you lower costs."
Ch.10 — Logistics management, organization, and the role of the logistics manager
Rushton Ch.10Planning

Logistics functions must be integrated across traditional departmental silos. The trend is toward process-driven organizational structures rather than functional silos. Cross-functional processes (order fulfilment, new product introduction) cut across departments.

Role of the logistics/distribution manager: Responsible for planning and managing the total logistics operation — transport, warehousing, inventory, and customer service. Acts as the interface between corporate strategy and operational execution.

Payment schemes in logistics: Moving from time-based (hourly/weekly pay) toward performance-based pay. Gainsharing (sharing of cost savings or profit above targets) aligns contractor incentives with client goals — a directly tested concept on your pre-assessment.

Traditional (functional)
Separate departments: Transport, Warehouse, Inventory, Customer Service. Each optimizes independently — creates silos.
Process-driven
Teams organized around customer-facing processes. Cross-functional. Breaks silos. Better total cost optimization.
Ch.11 — Multichannel fulfilment: omnichannel logistics challenges
Rushton Ch.11Planning

Multichannel (or omnichannel) fulfilment involves managing orders and deliveries across multiple sales channels simultaneously: physical stores, websites, mobile apps, click-and-collect, and marketplaces.

Key challenges:

  • Each channel has different order profiles: store orders are pallets, online orders are individual items
  • Returns management is more complex — customers return via any channel
  • Inventory visibility across all channels must be real-time
  • Last-mile delivery expectations from online shoppers are very high (next-day, same-day)
  • Food retailing multichannel is especially complex — ambient, chilled, frozen, and fresh all have different handling requirements

Omnichannel fulfillment system (OMS) manages orders across all channels from a single platform. This is one of the systems in the WMS/TMS/YMS/OMS family you need to know.

Ch.13 — Inventory: need to hold stocks, types, costs, EOQ, bullwhip
Rushton Ch.13 / Ch.15InventoryHIGH PRIORITY

Why hold stock? To: buffer against demand uncertainty, smooth production, cover supplier lead times, take advantage of purchase discounts, prevent stockouts.

Inventory types (Rushton's classification):

Cycle stock
Regular working stock to cover demand between replenishment orders. Depletes steadily and is replenished on a schedule.
Safety stock
Buffer held above expected demand to cover demand variability and supply uncertainty. Determined by service level target.
Pipeline stock
Inventory in transit — on the move between locations. Longer lead times = more pipeline stock.
Speculative stock
Held in anticipation of price rises or supply disruption. Forward buying.
Seasonal stock
Built up ahead of predictable demand peaks (Christmas, summer).
Dead/obsolescent stock
No longer sellable — superseded products, expired goods. Pure cost.

Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) is the reorder quantity that minimizes total inventory cost — balancing ordering costs (which fall with larger orders) against holding costs (which rise with larger orders). The EOQ formula: √(2DS/H) where D=annual demand, S=order cost, H=holding cost per unit per year.

The Bullwhip (Forrester) Effect: small variations in consumer demand are amplified as they pass upstream through the supply chain. Retailers over-order → wholesalers over-order more → manufacturers see wild swings. Causes: demand signal distortion, order batching, price fluctuations, rationing gaming. Mitigation: share actual demand data up the chain, reduce order batching, smooth pricing, reduce lead times.

Inventory cost elements: Capital cost (cost of money tied up), Storage cost (space, handling), Risk cost (obsolescence, damage, theft), Service cost (insurance, IT systems). Shortage cost is the cost of NOT having stock — lost sales, emergency procurement, customer penalties.
📚 Rushton — Chapters 25-29 (Freight Transport Modes)
Ch.25 — Modal choice: selecting the right transport mode
Rushton Ch.25Freight Transport

Modal choice is driven by four factors working together:

Operational factors
Origin-destination geography. Availability of infrastructure. Transit time requirements. Frequency of service needed.
Transport mode characteristics
Each mode's speed, capacity, reliability, flexibility, and safety record.
Consignment factors
Weight, volume, nature of goods (hazardous? perishable? fragile?), urgency.
Cost and service requirements
Total cost including freight, insurance, packaging, and inventory-in-transit.

Modal characteristics compared:

Air
Fastest. Most expensive per kg. Best for high-value, time-sensitive, low-weight goods. Limited by airport access.
Sea
Lowest cost per unit. Slowest. Best for bulk, heavy, non-urgent goods. Requires port access.
Rail
Reliable, lower emissions than road. Good for heavy bulk inland. Less flexible — fixed network.
Road
Most flexible — door to door. Dominates short/medium distance. Higher emissions than rail.
Ch.26 — Maritime transport: shipping terms, surcharges, documentation
Rushton Ch.26Freight Transport

Key maritime surcharges (directly tested):

BAF / Bunker Adjustment Factor
Accounts for variations in marine fuel (bunker) costs. The surcharge for marine fuel price changes.
CAF / Currency Adjustment Factor
Covers fluctuations in exchange rates between billing and payment currencies.
PSS / Peak Season Surcharge
Applied during high-demand periods when shipping capacity is tight.
GRI / General Rate Increase
Across-the-board freight rate increase applied periodically by carriers.

Container types: TEU (Twenty-foot Equivalent Unit) and FEU (Forty-foot Equivalent Unit) are the standard container size measures. A 40-foot container = 2 TEU = 1 FEU.

Key document: Bill of Lading (BL) = the main shipping document for sea freight. Acts as receipt for goods, evidence of contract of carriage, and document of title.

Common shipping terms (Incoterms): FOB (Free On Board) = seller's responsibility ends when goods are loaded on vessel. CIF (Cost, Insurance, Freight) = seller pays freight and insurance to destination port. DAP (Delivered At Place) = seller delivers to named destination.

Ch.27 — Air transport: structure, pricing, documentation, security
Rushton Ch.27Freight Transport

Air transport is governed by IATA (International Air Transport Association). Key document: Air Waybill (AWB) — the air freight equivalent of the Bill of Lading. Non-negotiable (unlike BL).

Air cargo pricing uses a weight/volume break system — freight is charged on actual weight OR volumetric weight, whichever is greater. Volumetric weight = (L × W × H in cm) ÷ 6000. This discourages shipping light, bulky items cheaply.

Hub-and-spoke system: Cargo from smaller airports is consolidated at major hubs (e.g. Frankfurt, Dubai, Memphis) before onward routing. This allows viable services between many city pairs that couldn't sustain direct flights.

Air cargo security: Post-9/11 security measures include: Known Shipper programs, X-ray screening, explosive detection. IATA's Cargo 2000 program sets quality standards for airfreight.

Ch.28 — Rail and intermodal transport
Rushton Ch.28Freight Transport

Intermodal transport moves cargo in a single loading unit (container or swap body) across multiple modes without handling the goods themselves when changing modes. The unit is transferred between truck, rail, and ship.

Intermodal equipment:

  • ISO container — standardized steel box, 20ft or 40ft, stackable, used on ships, rail wagons, and road vehicles
  • Swap body — European standard unit designed for road/rail interchange. Not stackable, lighter than ISO containers
  • Semi-trailer — road trailer that can be loaded onto a rail flatcar (piggyback/RoRo)

Rail transport advantages: Lower carbon emissions than road, suitable for heavy bulk over long distances, less affected by road congestion. Disadvantages: fixed routes, less flexibility, terminal handling adds time and cost.

Mode shift grants: Government incentives to shift freight from road to rail or water, reducing environmental impact.

Ch.29 — Road freight: vehicle types, costing, routing & scheduling
Rushton Ch.29Freight Transport

The two key factors for road vehicle selection (directly tested): weight and volume of cargo. These determine whether you need a high-cube body (for light, voluminous goods) or a standard body rated for maximum weight.

Main road vehicle types:

  • Articulated (artic) — tractor + semi-trailer. Maximum payload. UK max GVW ~44 tonnes.
  • Rigid — single-unit vehicle. More maneuverable. Various sizes 7.5t to 26t GVW.
  • Double-bottomed — two trailers behind a tractor. Long-distance efficiency (Australia, USA).

Vehicle costing — two categories:

Standing (fixed) costs
Depreciation, insurance, tax, driver base wage. Incurred regardless of how much the vehicle moves.
Running (variable) costs
Fuel, tyres, maintenance proportional to mileage. Only incurred when moving.

Vehicle routing and scheduling (VRS): The process of determining the optimal routes for a fleet of vehicles to serve a set of customer delivery points. Objectives: minimize total distance/time, maximize vehicle utilization, meet time windows. Computer-based systems use algorithms far superior to manual methods.

Primary vs secondary transport: Primary = trunking from factory/NDC to regional DCs (long haul, full loads). Secondary = local delivery from regional DC to customers (shorter runs, multiple drops).

📚 Rushton — Chapters 33,35,37 (Outsourcing & Environment)
Ch.33 — Outsourcing: services, types, drivers, and drawbacks
Rushton Ch.33Outsourcing

Outsourcing continuum: From full in-house logistics at one end, to fully integrated 4PL at the other. The key types along the spectrum:

Transport only
Hire carriers to move goods. Keep warehousing in-house. Simplest form of outsourcing.
Warehousing only
Use a 3PL warehouse. Keep transport in-house or use spot carriers.
Full 3PL
Transport + warehousing + value-added services under one contract.
4PL / Lead Logistics Provider
Manages entire supply chain. May not own assets. Orchestrates multiple 3PLs.

Key drivers for outsourcing: Cost reduction (access to 3PL's economies of scale), focus on core business, access to expertise and technology, flexibility (scale up/down without capital investment), geographic reach.

Key drawbacks: Loss of control over operations, dependency on provider, potential loss of in-house expertise, contract management overhead, brand/service risk if 3PL underperforms.

Critical factors in choosing a 3PL: Track record, financial stability, geographic coverage, IT capabilities, cultural fit, references from similar clients, cost competitiveness, and flexibility.

Ch.35 — Outsourcing management: monitoring, KPIs, relationship management
Rushton Ch.35Outsourcing

Why 3PL relationships fail (Rushton's key finding): poor communication, unclear performance expectations, cultural mismatch, insufficient management attention from the client, inadequate contract governance.

Monitoring an outsourced logistics operation requires:

  • Regular review meetings at multiple organizational levels (operational, management, strategic)
  • Open-book accounting — transparency of costs on both sides
  • Defined KPIs agreed at contract start, measured regularly
  • Corrective action process when KPIs are missed
  • Gainsharing mechanism when KPIs are exceeded

Implementation planning: The transition from in-house to outsourced must be carefully managed. Key risks: data migration errors, staff transfer issues (TUPE legislation), operational continuity during cutover.

📚 Rushton — Chapters 38,39,40,43 (Operational Management)
Ch.38 — Cost and performance monitoring: KPIs, balanced scorecard, control systems
Rushton Ch.38Ops Management

Why monitor? To identify where performance deviates from plan, enable corrective action, support management decision-making at all levels, and demonstrate logistics value to the business.

Approaches to performance measurement:

Balanced Scorecard
Four perspectives: Financial, Customer, Internal Process, Learning & Innovation. Gives balanced view beyond just cost.
SCOR Model
Supply Chain Operations Reference model. Standardized metrics: Plan, Source, Make, Deliver, Return.
Operating Control System
Compares actual performance to plan across all KPIs. Triggers corrective action when variance exceeds tolerance.

Hierarchy of needs principle: Different levels of management need different information. Board level = strategic metrics (total cost, service level trends). Middle management = departmental KPIs. Operational level = daily task metrics. The measurement system must be designed to meet each level's needs.

Validity principle: The operating control system data must cover all relevant stages of the logistics operation. If data is missing key stages (e.g., missing data on what happens in transit), the system lacks validity and decisions made from it will be flawed.

Key logistics KPIs include: OTIF (On Time In Full), order fill rate, inventory accuracy, cost per case/pallet/delivery, vehicle utilization, warehouse productivity (picks per hour), days without accident, customer complaints per thousand orders.

Ch.39 — Benchmarking: process, steps, formal systems

Why benchmark? To identify performance gaps vs best-in-class, set realistic improvement targets, understand what is operationally possible, and challenge internal assumptions about "good enough."

Key steps in a benchmarking project:

  1. Define what to measure (scope, KPIs)
  2. Collect internal performance data
  3. Identify benchmarking partners (same industry, different industry, or formal database)
  4. Collect external benchmark data
  5. Analyze gaps and identify root causes
  6. Develop improvement actions
  7. Implement and monitor progress

Formal benchmarking systems: Logmark (industry benchmarking database for logistics), Malcolm Baldridge National Quality Award criteria (US), ISO standards. Quality Function Deployment (QFD) focuses benchmarking on customer requirements driving supplier improvement.

Ch.40 — ICT in the supply chain: communication systems, WMS, TMS, RFID, e-commerce

Ch.43 (Rushton 5th ed. Ch.38) — Humanitarian logistics: key facts, differences, performance
Rushton Ch.43Humanitarian

Key differences between humanitarian and commercial logistics:

Commercial
Profit-driven. Known demand. Stable supply chains. Customer pays. KPIs = cost and service level.
Humanitarian
Need-driven. Unpredictable demand spikes. Disrupted supply chains. Donor-funded. KPIs = reach and speed of relief.

Key humanitarian logistics terms:

  • Pre-positioning: Storing relief supplies in strategic locations before disasters strike, to enable faster response
  • Cluster approach: UN system that assigns lead agencies to coordinate specific sectors (logistics cluster, health cluster) to avoid duplication
  • Extended delivery point (EDP): The furthest point in the supply chain where delivery can be tracked and confirmed
  • Last mile in humanitarian: Getting goods from distribution hubs to affected populations — often the hardest part

📚 Rushton — Chapters 18-24 (Warehousing Deep Dive)
Ch.18 (Rushton) — Warehouse operations: functions, roles, and costs
Rushton Ch.18Warehousing

Primary roles of warehouses: Buffer between supply and demand, consolidation point for multiple suppliers, break-bulk for redistribution, value-added services (labeling, kitting, assembly), returns processing, seasonal stock building.

Stockholding warehouse functions: Receiving → Put-away → Storage → Replenishment → Order picking → Packing → Dispatch. Each function has cost and productivity implications.

Cross-dock warehouse: No long-term storage. Goods transferred from inbound vehicles directly to outbound without extended storage. Key for perishables, promotional stock, and when speed is critical.

Warehouse costs breakdown: Labour (typically 40-60% of operating costs), space/building (20-30%), equipment (10-20%), systems and overheads (10%). Labour is the dominant cost — the justification for automation investment.

Rushton key point: Picking is the most labour-intensive function, representing 40-60% of all warehouse labour costs. This is why picking automation delivers the largest cost reduction.
Ch.19 (Rushton) — Palletized storage systems compared
Rushton Ch.19Warehousing

Palletized storage systems ranked by space utilization vs accessibility trade-off:

Block stacking
Pallets stacked on floor, no racking. Cheapest. Poor selectivity. LIFO only. Suitable for single-SKU runs.
Adjustable pallet racking (APR)
Standard beam racking with forklift access. Full selectivity (every pallet direct access). Most common system.
Drive-in / drive-through racking
Forklift drives into rack. Very high density. Drive-in = LIFO (one entrance). Drive-through = FIFO (two entrances).
Push-back racking
Pallets pushed back on inclined rails as new ones are added. LIFO. High density with some selectivity.
Pallet live storage
Gravity-fed roller lanes. FIFO. Good for date-sensitive products.
Narrow aisle (NA) racking
APR with very narrow aisles (1.6-1.8m). Specialist trucks. High space efficiency. Higher equipment cost.
AS/RS (automated)
Computer-controlled cranes store/retrieve pallets automatically. Highest capital cost. Best for high-volume, 24/7 operations.
Powered mobile racking
Racks on motorized rails — aisle is created on demand. Highest space utilization. High capital cost.
Ch.20 (Rushton) — Non-palletized storage: small items and long loads
Rushton Ch.20Warehousing

Small item storage systems:

  • Shelving — basic open shelving for manual pick. Cheapest, most flexible.
  • Carton live storage — gravity-fed lanes for small cartons. FIFO. Good for fast-moving pick faces.
  • Vertical carousels — motorized rotating shelves. Brings items to operator (goods-to-person). Reduces travel time.
  • Horizontal carousels — rotating oval track. Multiple units operated simultaneously by one picker for very high throughput.
  • Mini-load AS/RS — automated crane storage for small totes/bins. Highest throughput for small items.

Long loads storage — Cantilevered racking: Arms extending from upright posts with no front column obstruction. Specifically designed for long, awkward items: timber, steel bars, pipes, carpet rolls, turbine blades. Available in single-sided and double-sided configurations. Low investment cost relative to value it provides.

Exam answer: Long turbine blades + minimal investment cost = Cantilevered racking. Always.
Ch.21 (Rushton) — Order picking: concepts, methods, and productivity
Rushton Ch.21WarehousingHigh Priority

Order picking is the process of selecting products from storage to fulfil customer orders. It is the most labour-intensive warehouse activity and dominates warehouse cost.

Picking methods:

Single order picking
Pick one order at a time. Simple, accurate. Inefficient for high volume — lots of travel.
Batch picking
Pick multiple orders simultaneously in one pass. Reduces travel. Needs sortation afterwards.
Zone picking
Warehouse divided into zones, each picker handles their zone only. Orders assembled from zone picks. Reduces travel per picker.
Wave picking
All orders for a time window released at once. Coordinates multiple zones. Aligns picking with despatch slots.

Picking technologies: Paper pick list (traditional), RF handheld scanner, Pick-to-light (lights illuminate pick location), Voice-directed picking (spoken instructions, hands-free), Goods-to-person (automated system brings items to a pick station).

Slotting: Placing fast-moving items closest to despatch to minimize travel. A-items (Pareto top 20%) are slotted at golden zone height (waist to shoulder) nearest the despatch area. Proper slotting can reduce picking travel by 30-50%.

Picking productivity difference: Individual picker differences = picking method. Warehouse-to-warehouse differences = picking equipment usage. This distinction is directly tested.

Yard Management (Rushton) — bay assignment, vehicle scheduling, dock management
Rushton Yard MgmtWarehousing

Yard management covers the management of vehicles in the warehouse yard — from gate entry to dock assignment to departure. It's the interface between transport operations and warehouse operations.

Key functions:

  • Dock door assignment — allocating specific doors to inbound/outbound vehicles to optimize flow
  • Vehicle check-in — recording arrival, verifying booking, document check
  • Slot management — booking time windows for vehicle arrival to smooth workload
  • Yard movements — moving trailers within the yard, managing shunting tractors
  • Gate management — controlling entry/exit, security checks

Yard Management System (YMS) automates bay assignment and yard movements. Geofencing uses GPS to automatically detect vehicle arrival at the geofenced perimeter, triggering automated bay assignment and notifications — this is the technology for automating the bay assignment process.

Pre-assessment answer: "Automate assigning bays for loading/unloading vehicles" = Geofencing. The YMS manages yard operations broadly; geofencing is the specific triggering technology.
Ch.23 (Rushton) — Warehouse design: strategic issues and specialist warehouses
Rushton Ch.23Warehousing

Strategic issues in warehouse design:

  • Number and location of warehouses (from network planning)
  • Ownership vs rented: owned = more control, rented = more flexibility
  • Size: must accommodate current AND future requirements (5-10 year horizon)
  • Throughput profile: peak-to-average ratio determines building and equipment sizing
  • Automation level decision: justified by throughput volume and labour cost

Design procedure: Data collection → flow analysis → option development → evaluation → detailed design. Always start from data, not from preconceived solutions.

Specialist warehouse types:

Temperature-controlled (cold chain)
Chill (0-5°C), ambient chill (8-12°C), frozen (-18°C to -25°C). Pharma, food. High energy cost. Strict temp logging required.

High-security
For valuable goods: electronics, pharmaceuticals, jewellery. CCTV, access control, alarm systems.
Ch.24 (Rushton) — Warehouse management systems, data capture, and IT
Rushton Ch.24Warehousing

WMS core functions: Receiving, putaway (directs where to store), replenishment (from reserve to pick face), order picking (directs route and locations), packing, dispatch, inventory management, performance monitoring.

Data capture technologies in the warehouse:

Barcode (1D/2D)
Cheapest. Requires line-of-sight scan. Standard for most operations.
RFID
No line-of-sight needed. Read multiple tags simultaneously. Higher cost. Best for high-throughput dock doors.
Voice
Hands-free, eyes-free. Picker uses headset. Good for cold stores (gloves make scanning hard).

LED displays at locations show picker what to take. Fast and accurate. Fixed locations — less flexible.

Systems architecture: WMS integrates with ERP (for order data), TMS (for despatch/carrier booking), YMS (for dock scheduling), and customer-facing OMS. The WMS is the operational hub of the warehouse technology stack.

📚 Grainger — Cross-Border Logistics Operations (Chs 1,2,4,6,7,8)
Grainger Ch.1 — Cross-border logistics operations overview
Grainger Ch.1Cross-Border

Every border crossing exposes freight to trade and customs procedures. The UN recommended sign for border controls (a horizontal black line in a red circle) tells drivers they cannot pass without stopping — a symbol of the friction that borders impose on supply chains.

Cross-border logistics involves:

  • Prevailing trade, customs, and border policy
  • Tariffs and import taxes
  • Border management and supply chain security practices
  • Prohibitions and restrictions (what cannot cross)
  • Enforcement and compliance practices
  • Supply chain and logistics arrangements
  • Frictionless trade and trade facilitation principles

The goal of trade facilitation is to reduce the time, cost, and uncertainty of border crossings without compromising security or revenue collection. This is the fundamental tension in border management.

Grainger Ch.2 — Customs administrations: role, scope, and AEO programs
Grainger Ch.2Cross-Border

Customs administration role: Collect duties and taxes, enforce trade policy (prohibitions/restrictions), prevent smuggling, gather trade statistics, facilitate legitimate trade.

Customs procedures: Declaration → Assessment → Inspection (if selected) → Release. The Revised Kyoto Convention (RKC) is the international standard for modernizing customs procedures, aiming to make them more predictable, transparent, and simplified.

Authorized Economic Operator (AEO) / Trusted Trader programs: Businesses that demonstrate compliance, security, and financial solvency can apply for trusted trader status. Benefits include: faster clearance, fewer inspections, priority lanes, reduced data requirements. Non-compliance can disqualify businesses from these benefits — directly tested on your pre-assessment.

Customs valuation: The customs value is the basis for calculating import duties. The primary method is transaction value (what was actually paid). Alternative methods used when transaction value cannot be used.

Commodity code (HS code): The Harmonized System is the global product classification system used to determine tariff rates. Every product gets a 6-digit international code. Countries extend to 8-10 digits. The HS code defines the tariff rate — this is what "categorizes and defines customs tariff rates."
Grainger Ch.4 — Tariffs, taxes, and the cost of cross-border trade
Grainger Ch.4Cross-Border

Types of import duties/taxes:

Ad valorem tariff
A percentage of the customs value. Most common type. E.g., 5% of the declared CIF value.
Specific tariff
Fixed amount per unit (per kg, per litre, per item) regardless of value.
Anti-dumping duty
Additional duty applied when goods are imported at unfairly low prices that harm domestic industry.
VAT / GST
Value-added or goods and services tax applied on importation. Refundable for registered businesses in most jurisdictions.

Cross-border tariffs cost is the supply cost component specific to international trade — domestic operations don't pay import tariffs. This distinction is tested on the pre-assessment.

Production location determines country of origin, which determines applicable tariff rates and whether preferential trade agreement rates apply. Moving production to a different country can dramatically change import duty costs — which is why it affects customs tax liabilities.

Preferential tariff rates: Under Free Trade Agreements (FTAs) and customs unions, goods meeting Rules of Origin criteria can qualify for reduced or zero tariffs between member countries.

Grainger Ch.6 — Enforcement, compliance, and the FCPA
Grainger Ch.6Cross-Border

Enforcement mechanisms: Risk profiling selects shipments for inspection (not every shipment can be inspected). Non-intrusive inspection (NII) uses X-ray and scanning technology. Physical examination is resource-intensive and creates delay.

Compliance: Importers/exporters must comply with all relevant regulations — customs, import restrictions, product standards, sanctions, export controls. Non-compliance consequences include: fines, seizure of goods, criminal prosecution, and loss of trusted trader (AEO) status.

Foreign Corrupt Practices Act (FCPA) 1977: US law prohibiting bribery of foreign government officials by US persons, entities, and their agents worldwide. Key characteristics:

  • Applies to US corporations AND individuals — not just corporations
  • Covers payments made both inside AND outside the US
  • Applies to unsanctioned payments made to obtain or retain business
  • Multinational supply chains create FCPA exposure when operating in high-risk countries
Pre-assessment trap: Wrong answers say "corporations but not individuals" or "outside the country only." The FCPA applies to BOTH corporations AND individuals, and covers payments INSIDE AND OUTSIDE the country.
Grainger Ch.7 — Supply chain security: C-TPAT, AEO, BEST, and risk management
Grainger Ch.7Cross-Border

Post-9/11 security measures have significantly changed international logistics. The challenge is balancing security inspection with trade facilitation speed.

Key supply chain security programs:

C-TPAT (US)
Customs-Trade Partnership Against Terrorism. US program for trusted importers/exporters/carriers. Members get expedited clearance.
AEO (EU)
Authorized Economic Operator. EU equivalent of C-TPAT. Mutual recognition agreements exist between programs.
BEST
Border Enforcement Security Task Force. US inter-agency group specifically focused on improving SAFETY in international trade and supply chain. Directly tested on pre-assessment.
CSI
Container Security Initiative. US CBP program to screen high-risk containers at foreign ports BEFORE they are loaded on US-bound vessels.

Supply chain security risk management: Identify threats → assess vulnerability → implement countermeasures → monitor and review. Physical security (seals, locks, CCTV), information security (access controls, data protection), and personnel security (screening, training) all matter.

Grainger Ch.8 — Trade facilitation: TFA, Single Window, frictionless trade
Grainger Ch.8Cross-Border

Trade Facilitation Agreement (TFA): The WTO agreement (entered into force 2017) that legally binds all WTO members to implement streamlined, transparent, and predictable customs procedures. The first new WTO multilateral agreement in 20 years. Directly tested: "international law harmonizing customs procedures worldwide" = TFA.

Revised Kyoto Convention (RKC): The World Customs Organization's (WCO) blueprint for modern customs procedures. Goal = make customs more predictable. Standards for simplified declarations, advance rulings, appeals, and trusted trader programs.

Single Window: A national trade facilitation platform where traders submit all import/export documentation once, through a single portal, rather than to multiple agencies separately. Eliminates duplication and speeds clearance.

Freight forwarder role: The freight forwarder prepares and submits customs declarations on behalf of importers/exporters. They are the key intermediary in the customs compliance process, managing documentation, arranging inspection appointments, and ensuring compliance.

Improving declarations and documentation is the practice that most directly speeds border clearance — accurate, complete paperwork enables customs to risk-assess and release quickly.

📚 Rushton — Chapters 15,16,17 (Inventory Deep Dive)
Ch.15 (WGU numbering) — Inventory planning: reorder systems, safety stock, EOQ
Rushton InventoryInventory

Inventory replenishment systems:

Periodic review
Inventory checked at fixed intervals (weekly, monthly). Order placed at each review to bring stock up to target level. Simple, predictable ordering pattern.
Fixed reorder point (ROP)
Order triggered when stock falls to a predetermined level (the reorder point). Order quantity is fixed. More responsive to demand variation.

Safety stock calculation: Safety stock = Z × σ × √(L) where Z = service level factor, σ = demand standard deviation, L = lead time. Higher service level = more safety stock needed. Longer lead time = more safety stock needed.

Service level and safety stock trade-off: 95% service level requires ~1.65 standard deviations of safety stock. 99% service level requires ~2.33. The relationship is non-linear — getting from 95% to 99% costs much more than getting from 90% to 95%.

Reorder point (ROP) = Average demand during lead time + Safety stock. When inventory hits the ROP, place the next order.

EOQ formula: √(2DS/H) where D = annual demand units, S = ordering cost per order, H = holding cost per unit per year. As order size increases, holding cost goes up, ordering cost per unit goes down. EOQ is where these cross.

Ch.16 (WGU) — Push/pull systems, lead-time gap, decoupling point
Rushton Ch.14Inventory

Lead-time gap: The difference between the total supply lead time (from raw materials to finished goods) and the customer order lead time (how long customers will wait). When the lead-time gap is positive, the supply chain cannot respond fast enough to customer orders — so inventory must be held to bridge the gap.

Decoupling point: The point in the supply chain where make-to-stock (push) transitions to make-to-order (pull). Upstream of the decoupling point, goods are produced based on forecasts. Downstream, production is triggered by actual orders.

Push (MTS)
Make to Stock. Production driven by forecast. Risk: overstock if forecast wrong. Benefit: fast availability.
Pull (MTO)
Make to Order. Production triggered by actual order. Risk: lead time too long for customer. Benefit: no excess stock.
Postponement
Generic products made in advance (push), final customization deferred until order received (pull). Reduces variety risk.

Just-in-time (JIT) is the extreme pull system — nothing is produced or moved until the customer or next process needs it. Minimizes inventory. Requires very reliable suppliers and short lead times. Highly vulnerable to supply disruption.

CPFR (Collaborative Planning, Forecasting and Replenishment) — retailer and supplier share demand forecasts and plans to reduce the bullwhip effect and improve availability. Benetton's quick response system is cited by Rushton as a classic example of lead-time compression through supply chain collaboration.

Ch.17 (WGU) — Demand forecasting: qualitative and quantitative methods
Rushton Ch.13Inventory

Qualitative forecasting methods (used when data is limited or a new product is launching):

  • Delphi method — experts make independent forecasts, share results, revise, repeat until consensus
  • Market research / consumer survey — asking customers about purchase intentions
  • Sales force composite — aggregating salespeople's estimates of what customers will buy
  • Analogous products — using a similar product's history as a proxy

Quantitative forecasting methods (use historical data):

Moving average
Simple average of the last N periods. Smooths short-term fluctuations. Equal weight to all N periods. "Average of past 2 years actual demand" = moving average.
Exponential smoothing
Weighted average giving more weight to recent data. Adjusts automatically. Alpha (α) = smoothing factor. High α = more weight to recent data.
Causal / regression
Uses external variables (advertising spend, economic indices, weather) as predictors. Good when strong external driver exists.
Seasonal decomposition
Separates trend, seasonal pattern, and random variation. Best for products with strong seasonal patterns.

Elements of a demand pattern: Trend (long-term direction), Seasonality (regular periodic fluctuation), Cyclicality (longer economic cycles), Random variation (unexplained noise).

Exam answer: "Average of past N periods' actual demand" = Moving average. "More weight to recent data" = Exponential smoothing. "External variable drives demand" = Causal/regression.
