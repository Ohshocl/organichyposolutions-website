Section 1 — Elements of Distribution, Logistics & Transportation
Logistics vs Distribution vs Supply Chain — scope hierarchy
S1 Core

Logistics = planning and managing the flow of goods, services, and information from origin to consumption. It's the broader discipline that encompasses transportation, warehousing, and inventory.

Distribution = the subset of logistics focused specifically on getting finished goods from manufacturer/warehouse out to customers. Multiple distribution channels and alternate ways of delivering products exist.

Supply Chain = the widest scope — includes all parties and processes from raw material suppliers through manufacturers to end customers. Logistics is one function within supply chain management.

Effective managers integrate logistics into the distribution system in relation to logistics management. Logistics strategy is derived from the corporate business plan — it doesn't exist independently.
Logistics design strategies — 4 characteristics of distribution system development
S1 CoreQ1 Q12

There are four main characteristics of a product that affect distribution system development: product value, product density (volume-to-weight), product handling requirements, and demand risk.

Operational decisions that affect a shipping company's functionality are decisions about day-to-day running — vehicle maintenance is an operational decision (not strategic). Location/size of depots = strategic/tactical. Fleet capacity management = tactical.

Operational
Day-to-day: Vehicle maintenance, picking assignments, scheduling
Strategic
Long-term: Location/size of depots, network design, fleet composition
Q1 Trap: "Which operational decision affects a shipping company's functionality?" Answer = Vehicle maintenance. "Location and size of depots" is strategic/tactical, not operational.
Logistics providers — 1PL, 2PL, 3PL, 4PL
S1 CoreQ2 Q3
3PL
Third-party logistics: provides transportation, warehousing, specific parts of supply chain. Outsourcing specific functions.
4PL
Fourth-party logistics: manages the ENTIRE supply chain. Orchestrates 3PLs. Solutions delivered across the entire supply chain — not just specific parts.

Why outsource? Primary reason = to reduce logistics costs. Not to increase assets, not to make logistics a core competency (outsourcing does the opposite — it removes it from core).

3PL = specific parts. 4PL = entire supply chain. 4PL is NOT the same as LLP (Lead Logistics Provider) — though similar, they are distinct terms.
Transportation modes — choosing the right one
S1 CoreQ4 Q5 Q6

Air = optimal for high-value, time-sensitive, fragile items over long distances. An expensive antique vase shipped a very long distance = Air. Why: speed + security + handling standards for fragile items.

Road transport selection factors: The two key factors for selecting the appropriate road transport type for a large shipment = weight and volume of cargo. Not length/breadth, not size/shape.

Air
High-value, fragile, time-sensitive, long distance. Fastest. Most expensive per kg.
Sea
Bulk, heavy, non-urgent. Cheapest per unit. Slowest.
Rail
Heavy bulk inland. Cost-effective for large volumes overland.
Road
Flexible, door-to-door, short-medium distance. Most common for last-mile.

Marine fuel surcharge: Variations in marine (sea) fuel pricing are accounted for by the Bunker Adjustment Surcharge (BAS). Not carrier security, not peak season, not currency adjustment.

Volume-to-weight ratio — distribution efficiency
S1 CoreQ7

Distribution efficiency is measured by the volume-to-weight ratio. A lower ratio means the product is dense and heavy relative to its size — it fills weight capacity before space capacity, making each trip more efficient.

Given ratios: A=100, B=600, C=1000, D=250 — Product A (ratio=100) is most efficient because you can load the most weight per unit of space. Low ratio = dense = efficient.

Rule: Lowest volume-to-weight ratio = most efficient for distribution. The product fits the most weight into the least space per trip.
Product Life Cycle (PLC) — stages and sales behavior
S1 CoreQ8 Q13 Q14
Introductory
Sales increase at an increasing rate — market is discovering the product. Steep positive slope beginning.
Growth
Greatest positive slope — fastest rate of sales increase. Market adoption accelerating rapidly.
Maturity
Sales increase at a decreasing rate — growth slowing. Competition peaks.
Decline
Sales decrease. Product becoming obsolete or replaced.
Q14 Trap: "What happens to sales in the INTRODUCTORY phase?" = They increase at an INCREASING rate. Students confuse this with Growth (which has the steepest slope). Growth = greatest positive slope. Introductory = increasing but not yet at peak slope.

Own-brand substitution (supermarket producing its own identical products when branded stock runs low) = decreases logistics costs. It reduces supplier dependence and simplifies the supply chain.

Operations strategies — the 4 Vs
S1 CoreQ9 Q11

When a company faces large fluctuations in demand, it should use a Variability-based production strategy — designed specifically to handle demand uncertainty and variation.

Volume-based
High-volume, standardized production. Economies of scale. Low variety.
Variety-based
Wide product range. High customization. Lower volumes per SKU.
Variability-based
High demand fluctuation. Agile production. Adjusts output to variable demand.
Visibility-based
Customer can see/track their product being made. Services.

Which performance type minimizes waste? = Quality performance. Quality-focused operations eliminate defects and rework, which are forms of waste. Not time (speed), not flexibility, not dependability.

Manufacturing processes — matching process to product
S1 CoreQ10

A large chemical company producing water treatment chemicals for municipal facilities should use Continuous production — because the product is identical, high-volume, non-stop, and requires a consistent uninterrupted process.

Project
One-off unique items. Long duration. Ship, building, custom machine.
Batch
Groups of similar items. Bakery, pharmaceuticals in batches.
Mass
High volume, standardized. Automotive assembly.
Continuous
Non-stop 24/7. Chemicals, oil refining, paper mills. Identical product, never stops.
Logistics network reengineering — cost vs service tradeoff
S1 CoreQ12

When a company reengineers its logistics network by lowering costs, the consequence is that the number of facilities decreases. Fewer warehouses/distribution centers = lower fixed costs, but typically results in longer delivery lead times and lower responsiveness.

Cost-service tradeoff: Lowering costs → fewer facilities → lower service level (longer lead times, less responsiveness). Increasing service → more facilities → higher costs.
Logistics types — Inbound, Outbound, Reverse
S1 CoreQ15
Inbound
Materials/goods coming INTO the organization from suppliers. Procurement logistics.
Outbound
Finished goods going OUT to customers. Distribution logistics.
Reverse
Returns, recycling, waste management. Goods flowing backwards through supply chain. Company improving recycling = Reverse logistics.
Distribution operations — Break-bulk, Cross-docking, Postponement
S1 CoreQ16 Q37
Break-bulk
Receives large shipments, breaks them into smaller ones for distribution. Large in → small out.
Cross-docking
Goods transferred directly from inbound to outbound with no storage. Minimizes warehouse time.
Postponement
Delaying final configuration/customization until closer to demand point.
Echelon
Multi-level distribution: production → warehouse → local DCs → customers. Sequential layers.

Distribution process moving goods from production → warehouse → local DCs → customers = Echelon distribution (multi-level sequential flow).

Government transport actions — Incentive vs Regulation vs Control
S1 CoreQ17

A government setting a road tax exemption for electric vehicles = Incentive. It encourages desired behavior through a benefit/reward, not by mandating compliance.

Incentive
Rewards desired behavior (tax exemptions, subsidies, grants). Voluntary. EV road tax exemption.
Regulation
Mandatory rules with penalties for non-compliance. Emissions standards, speed limits.
Control
Direct government management/ownership of transport infrastructure.
Target
Policy goals/objectives without specific enforcement mechanism.
Empty miles — minimizing backhaul waste
S1 CoreQ18 Q36

The most efficient way to minimize empty miles = Pick up lots from suppliers AFTER drop-off to customers. This eliminates the return trip being empty by using it productively.

Separately: A vehicle returning empty after every delivery can be optimized by collecting raw materials on the return trip — same principle, backhaul utilization.

Backhaul optimization: Never run empty. Use the return journey to collect from suppliers. Combines delivery and pickup into one trip = half the empty miles.
Section 2 — Laws, Regulations & Cross-Border Operations
Cross-border logistics decisions affecting customs tax liability
S2 CoreQ19

The cross-border decision that affects customs tax liabilities = Production location. Where goods are produced determines country of origin, which determines tariff rates and preferential trade agreements.

Country of origin is tied to production location → affects which tariff schedule applies → directly impacts customs tax liability. Production lot size = inventory decision. Production quality/reliability = operations decisions unrelated to customs.
Customs agencies — who does what
S2 CoreQ20 Q31
Freight Forwarder
Prepares and submits customs declarations on behalf of importers/exporters. Acts as intermediary with customs authorities.
BEST
Border Enforcement Security Task Force — improves SAFETY in international trade and supply chain.
Terminal Operator
Manages port/terminal facilities. Loading/unloading of vessels.
Payment Bank
Handles letters of credit and payment in international trade. Not customs.
International trade laws — TFA, Kyoto Convention, FCPA
S2 CoreQ21 Q22 Q26

Trade Facilitation Agreement (TFA) = the international law that focuses on harmonizing customs procedures throughout the world. WTO agreement streamlining global customs processes.

Kyoto Customs Convention goal = to make customs procedures more predictable. It creates transparency, simplification, and standardization of customs procedures — NOT to control businesses or build opacity.

Corrupt Practice Act / FCPA (1977) = involves the use of corporations making unsanctioned payments both inside AND outside the country. It prohibits bribing foreign government officials. Key point: applies to corporations AND individuals, and covers both domestic and international unsanctioned payments.

FCPA Trap: One wrong answer says "multinational corporations but not individuals" — WRONG. The FCPA applies to individuals too.
Customs tariff rates — commodity codes
S2 CoreQ23

The factor that helps define and categorize customs tariff rates = Commodity code (also called Harmonized System code or HS code). Every product gets a numerical code that determines its tariff rate.

Commodity code (HS code) = the universal numerical classification system for goods. The code determines tariff rates, not the transportation mode or customer code. Goods valuation also matters but commodity classification comes first.
Border clearance best practices
S2 CoreQ24

To assist international agencies at the border and ensure efficient clearance of products = Improving declarations and documentation. Accurate, complete paperwork is the primary enabler of smooth border crossings.

Customs authorities need correct documentation to process shipments. Poor or incomplete declarations are the #1 cause of delays. "Improving declarations and documentation" directly addresses this.
Customs & Trade Compliance Manager — key skills
S2 CoreQ25

A customs and trade compliance manager needs = The ability to ensure successful systems deployment for vigorous trade operations. This role manages technology systems, processes, and regulatory compliance that enable efficient cross-border trade.

Supply cost specific to international trade
S2 CoreQ27

The key supply cost component specific to international trade = Cross-border tariffs cost. Tariffs are unique to international trade — domestic trade has no equivalent. Rework cost, quality cost, and direct taxation exist in domestic operations too.

Agile supply chain strategies — Quick Response, ECR
S2 CoreQ28 Q29

Supply chain strategy used to address demand changes using an agile method = Quick Response. Quick Response shortens replenishment lead times and increases responsiveness to actual demand.

Efficient Consumer Response (ECR) benefit in global trade = Improved service level. ECR focuses on improving the efficiency of the entire supply chain from manufacturer to consumer, primarily measured by service level improvement.

Quick Response
Agile strategy. Short replenishment cycles. Fast reaction to demand signals. Apparel industry origin.
ECR
Efficient Consumer Response. Grocery industry origin. Focuses on supply chain efficiency + consumer service level.
Border control challenges for garment manufacturing
S2 CoreQ30

A country with poor border control mechanisms creates a challenge for garment manufacturers of = Increased product loss in the market. Weak border controls allow counterfeit goods, piracy, and unofficial imports to undermine legitimate products.

Section 3 — Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) & Operations Management
Inventory types — Cycle, Safety, Pipeline, In-process, Speculative
S3 CoreQ33 Q34 Q38 Q39
Cycle Stock
Stock equivalent to typical regular demand — meets frequent customer purchases for a set period. Q34: one quarter of regular demand = Cycle stock.
Safety Stock
Buffer stock held to protect against demand uncertainty or supply delays. Extra above cycle stock.
In-process
Partially finished stock held close to production for rapid completion and dispatch. Q33: WIP held at nearby supplier for 1-hour delivery = In-process.
Pipeline
Stock in transit between locations. "On the way" inventory.
Speculative
Stock bought in advance anticipating price rises or shortages.
Seasonal
Built up ahead of predictable seasonal demand peaks.

Inventory cost driver: A firm rapidly increasing its variety of goods (needing more SKUs) → rising inventory costs = Increased product proliferation. More product lines = more SKUs = more inventory to hold.

Shortage cost: Penalty for not holding adequate stock = Shortage cost (element of inventory-holding cost).

Forecasting methods — Moving Average, Exponential Smoothing, Causal
S3 CoreQ32

Calculating average demand for the next period based on actual demand from the previous two years = Moving Average. It averages historical data over a defined time window.

Moving Average
Average of actual demand over a fixed historical period. Simple, uses past data directly.
Exponential Smoothing
Weighted average giving more weight to recent data. Automatically adjusts for trends.
Causal
Uses external variables (weather, GDP, advertising spend) to predict demand.
Experimental
Based on market testing / consumer research. Qualitative.
Pareto analysis — A/B/C line classification
S3 CoreQ35

Products sold quickly at 90% availability across all DCs = A line in Pareto analysis.

A Line
Top 20% of products generating ~80% of revenue. Fastest moving. Highest availability target (90%+). Priority at all DCs.
B Line
Mid-tier. Regular movers. Moderate availability (75-85%).
C Line
Slow movers. 50% of SKUs, ~5% of revenue. Lower availability targets, fewer locations.
D Line
Obsolete or nearly discontinued. Minimal stock.
Operations control systems — principles and benchmarking
S3 CoreQ40 Q41 Q42 Q43

Operating control system = evaluates KPIs related to cost centers and operations standards, compares actual vs planned. Tracks performance variance and drives corrective action.

Validity = the principle that data covers ALL stages of production and distribution necessary for decisions. When data is missing stages = Validity is compromised.

Measurement system best practice when managers at different decision levels contribute = Hierarchy of needs. Design the system around the decision-making levels.

Benchmarking = comparing your performance against world-class standards from other industries to identify gaps and drive continuous improvement.

Malcolm Baldridge National Quality Award criteria = the US framework based on productivity and operational performance excellence officially recognized in the United States.

Key benchmarking terms: Benchmarking = comparing vs best-in-class. Quality Function Deployment = benchmarking system focused on customer requirements and supplier performance.
Outsourcing logistics — 3PL decisions and impacts
S3 CoreQ52 Q53 Q54 Q55 Q56 Q57 Q58 Q59

Impact of outsourcing distribution/logistics: Focusing on core business = decreases logistics decision-making control (you give up control when you outsource). Investment freed up = investment opportunities in more profitable business areas.

Multiuser 3PL advantage: Serves multiple clients = economies of scale can be achieved with other firms (shared assets reduce per-unit cost).

Risk of outsourcing to unknown start-up: Strong-brand firm + unknown 3PL = the third-party brand could interfere with the firm's brand. Customer experience is affected by the 3PL's reputation.

First step when outsourcing operations: = Identify service requirements and costs. Before selecting a provider, you must define what you need and what it should cost.

Dedicated 3PL charges more because of: = Specialization of knowledge and expertise. When a provider serves one client exclusively, they develop deep specialized expertise worth a premium.

Gainsharing: Provider gets % of profits when exceeding targets = Gainsharing-based activity incentive. Aligns provider incentives with client performance goals.

Performance tracking in outsourcing: Monitoring targets via open-book audits with decision-makers = Performance tracking.

Section 4 — Distribution & Warehouse Management
Warehouse management systems (WMS) — which system for which task
S4 CoreQ44 Q60 Q70
WMS
Controls all warehouse activities: automated equipment, RF communications, picking, put-away, inventory. Q44 and Q70.
TMS
Transportation Management System. Plans and optimizes shipping routes, carrier selection, freight audit.
YMS
Yard Management System. Controls vehicle movements in the yard, dock assignments.
OMS
Order Management System. Customer-facing order processing, multi-channel orders.

Automated storage/retrieval system (AS/RS) = connects picking operations to WMS to eliminate human intervention. Fully automated storage and retrieval.

Geofencing = automates the process of assigning bays for loading/unloading vehicles based on GPS location.

Racking and storage systems — matching to requirement
S4 CoreQ61
Cantilevered Racking
For long, awkward items (timber, pipes, turbine blades). Arms extend from upright with no front column — accommodates length. Minimal investment cost.
Powered Mobile Racking
High-density storage. Aisles on wheels. Highest cost, best space utilization.
Drive-in/Drive-through
Forklift drives into racking. LIFO (drive-in) or FIFO (drive-through). Bulk storage.
AS/RS
Automated Storage/Retrieval. High capital cost. Fully automated with WMS.
Picking — most labor-intensive warehouse activity
S4 CoreQ62 Q63 Q64

Picking = the most labor-intensive and cost-intensive warehouse activity. A company wanting to reduce direct labor costs should focus on picking first.

Why do two pickers have different productivity (100 vs 200 lines/hour)? = Picking method. Different methods (single order, batch, zone, wave picking) produce dramatically different productivity rates for individual pickers.

Why do two WAREHOUSES have different average picking productivity (X vs 2X orders/picker/hour)? = Picking equipment usage. At warehouse level, equipment (conveyors, pick-to-light, voice-directed, automated systems) explains the gap, not individual method.

Trap: Pickers (individual level) → Picking method. Warehouses (facility level) → Picking equipment usage. Don't confuse the level of analysis.
Warehouse planning horizons and metrics
S4 CoreQ65 Q66 Q67

Short-term planning = Workload planning based on order receipts. Responding to actual orders received = short-term (daily/weekly). Budget planning = long-term. Capacity planning = medium-term. Demand planning = medium-term.

Metric to improve warehouse employee safety = Days without an accident. Tracks safety performance directly. Stock integrity = accuracy metric. Customer service levels = service metric.

Put-away cycle time and stock turnover ratio calculations
S4 CoreQ68 Q69

Put-away cycle time = time from stock RECEIPT to STORAGE LOCATION (not from order receipt). Stock received 12th, moved to storage 15th = 3 days put-away cycle time. (15 - 12 = 3).

Q68 Trap: Don't include the order processing time (16th-17th). Put-away = receipt to storage only. Stock received day 12, stored day 15 = 3 days.

Stock turnover ratio = how many times inventory is sold/used in a year. Higher ratio = lower inventory held (turns over faster). W=4, X=5, Y=8, Z=10 → Warehouse Z holds the lowest inventory (turns over 10x/year = very lean).

Higher stock turnover = lower inventory = leaner operation. Z turns over 10x = holds the least stock on hand at any time.
